# When Faith Becomes Law: Christianity, Political Power, and the Limits of Religious Authority

## Abstract

Christianity has played a defining yet deeply contested role in shaping the political, legal, and cultural development of the United States and much of the wider world. Its influence can be seen in moral philosophy, civic language, education, reform movements, charity, abolitionist thought, civil rights activism, and concepts of human dignity. Yet this influence has never been politically neutral. When Christianity moves from moral persuasion into governmental authority, it risks becoming not a source of conscience but an instrument of control. The central concern is not the presence of faith in public life, but the transformation of faith into law—particularly when one religious tradition is used to define public morality, restrict individual rights, and determine who fully belongs within the political community.

This article examines the tension between Christianity as a moral and cultural force and Christianity as a vehicle of political power. It argues that Christianity becomes most dangerous in public life when political leaders and religious movements use it to claim authority while disregarding the ethical demands of the faith itself. In such cases, religion becomes performative: a public symbol of legitimacy, loyalty, and identity rather than a consistent moral practice. This dynamic is especially visible in forms of politicized evangelicalism and Christian nationalism, where religious language is used to frame political opposition as moral corruption and to justify laws governing sexuality, gender, education, reproduction, and public identity.

The problem extends beyond Christianity alone. Across religious traditions, faith becomes coercive when it is fused with state power and used to impose one group’s beliefs on those outside the tradition. However, in the American context, Christianity occupies a uniquely influential position because of its long association with national identity and political legitimacy. This article contends that democratic society must distinguish between religious participation in public life and religious domination through law. Faith may guide individuals, communities, and moral debate, but when it becomes an instrument of state authority, it threatens pluralism, equality, and the very religious freedom it claims to defend.

# Introduction: When Faith Becomes Political Authority

Christianity has never been merely a private belief system in the United States. It has lived in churches, homes, schools, courtrooms, campaign speeches, hospitals, protest movements, family expectations, public ceremonies, scientific debates, medical ethics, and national myths. It has shaped how people understand morality, justice, suffering, charity, punishment, gender, sexuality, authority, knowledge, the body, and belonging. To pretend Christianity has not influenced American public life would require a level of denial usually reserved for family group chats and politicians caught on camera. Its presence is undeniable. The harder question is what happens when that presence becomes power.

This article begins from a distinction that is often ignored: faith is not the same thing as law, and moral influence is not the same thing as political authority. Christianity, like many religious traditions, can offer meaning, discipline, compassion, hope, and a framework for understanding human dignity. It has inspired people to feed the hungry, care for the sick, resist slavery, march for civil rights, build communities, and speak against injustice. At its best, Christianity has given language to the wounded and courage to the powerless. It has helped people survive grief, organize resistance, and imagine a world less cruel than the one they inherited.

But Christianity has also been used for purposes far removed from humility, mercy, or love of neighbor. It has been used to justify conquest, slavery, segregation, patriarchy, censorship, forced assimilation, anti-LGBTQ politics, reproductive control, suppression of inquiry, and the punishment of those considered morally deviant. That contradiction is not a minor historical footnote. It is the wound at the center of Christianity’s political life. The same tradition that has inspired liberation has also been used to bless domination. The same scriptures used to preach justice have also been quoted to defend hierarchy. The same faith that tells believers to care for the vulnerable has been used by some political movements to make the vulnerable even more vulnerable. The irony is not subtle. It is wearing a nametag.

The issue, then, is not whether Christianity should exist in public life. Religious people do not stop being religious when they vote, protest, organize, write, speak, or serve in office. Nor should they be expected to. A democratic society must allow people to bring their deepest convictions into public debate, including religious convictions. Faith can shape conscience, and conscience has always shaped politics. The question is not whether Christians may participate in democracy. Of course they may. The question is whether Christianity, or any particular interpretation of it, should be allowed to become the moral machinery of the state.

That distinction matters because law does what belief alone cannot: it compels. A sermon may persuade, comfort, challenge, or offend. A law can punish. A church may define sin for its members. A government can restrict rights, regulate bodies, censor speech, control education, fund or defund research, and decide who receives protection. When Christianity moves from moral persuasion into legal enforcement, the relationship between faith and freedom changes. What may begin as religious conviction can become political coercion, especially for those who do not share the faith being enforced. In that moment, Christianity no longer functions only as a source of spiritual guidance. It becomes a governing instrument.

This transformation is especially visible in the rise of politicized Christianity, particularly in forms of evangelical politics and Christian nationalism that present one version of Christianity as the natural or rightful foundation of American identity. In these movements, Christianity often becomes less a demanding ethical tradition and more a public badge of loyalty. It signals who belongs, who is trustworthy, who is “real,” and who must be corrected, converted, or controlled. Religious language becomes a political password. Say the right words, invoke the right symbols, gesture toward the right moral panic, and suddenly political power is dressed up as divine purpose. It is not always faith leading politics. Sometimes it is politics wearing faith’s clothes and hoping no one notices the tailoring is awful.

This performative use of Christianity is one of the central problems examined in this article. Political leaders often invoke Christianity not because they are committed to its ethical demands, but because it grants moral cover. It can make cruelty sound righteous, exclusion sound protective, and control sound like freedom. The language of faith becomes a shield against criticism: if a policy is framed as religious, then opposing it can be portrayed as opposing God, morality, family, or the nation itself. This is how political disagreement becomes spiritual warfare. This is how citizens become enemies. This is how public policy becomes a pulpit with police power.

This selective use of religious authority becomes especially revealing when some biblical teachings are treated as flexible and contextual while others are treated as absolute, timeless, and suitable for law. Many religious communities already interpret scripture through modern circumstances when doing so protects social stability, pastoral compassion, or the comfort of the dominant group. That flexibility is not necessarily the problem; interpretation is part of every living religious tradition. The problem is pretending that flexibility does not exist while denying it to others. When divorce, wealth, violence, poverty, or political cruelty can be approached with nuance, but sexuality, gender, reproduction, and family structure are treated as rigid battlegrounds for legal control, the result is not consistent theology. It is moral convenience dressed as conviction. And the outfit is not as flattering as it thinks.

The same pattern appears in conflicts over knowledge and scientific innovation. The Galileo affair remains a lasting example of church authority attempting to discipline inquiry when emerging evidence threatened established theological interpretation and institutional power; in 1633, Galileo was convicted of “vehement suspicion of heresy,” and his sentence was commuted to house arrest [@stanford_galileo]. Modern debates over stem-cell research reveal a related tension, though with important distinctions. Religious opposition, particularly from Catholic teaching, has focused most strongly on embryonic stem-cell research because of claims about embryos, personhood, and the beginning of human life, while other forms of stem-cell research, including adult stem-cell research, have often been treated differently [@vatican_dignitas_personae; @nih_stem_cell_basics]. The issue is not that religion has no place in ethical debate over science. It should. The issue is what happens when doctrine is allowed to police inquiry, restrict public funding, or limit medical possibility for people who do not share the same theological premises. At that point, faith is no longer merely asking moral questions of science; it is attempting to set the boundaries of knowledge itself.

The emotional force of this issue lies in its consequences. The debate is not abstract for the people who live under laws shaped by religious authority they do not share. It is not abstract for women whose bodies become legislative battlegrounds. It is not abstract for LGBTQ people whose identities are treated as threats to civilization. It is not abstract for patients and families waiting on medical research that becomes politically trapped in theological dispute. It is not abstract for religious minorities who are told, subtly or openly, that they are guests in a nation imagined as Christian property. It is not abstract for atheists, agnostics, progressive Christians, or anyone whose conscience does not fit inside the approved political theology of the moment. When faith becomes law, the people outside that faith do not experience it as inspiration. They experience it as control.

Yet this critique is not an argument against Christianity itself. It is an argument against the corruption of faith by power. In fact, one could argue that limiting religious authority within government protects religion as much as it protects democracy. When the state adopts religious language for political ends, religion is not elevated; it is cheapened. It becomes campaign merchandise, a slogan, a loyalty test, a weapon. A faith tradition rooted in sacrifice, humility, mercy, and care for the oppressed is flattened into a political brand. That should concern believers and nonbelievers alike. No religion is made holier by being handed the machinery of the state. Power does not purify faith. More often, it teaches faith how to enjoy the throne.

The American context is particularly important because Christianity has long been tied to national identity, even in a constitutional system that rejects the establishment of an official religion. This tension has produced a strange and persistent contradiction: a country that formally protects religious freedom has often culturally treated Christianity as the default setting. That default has shaped whose morality becomes law, whose holidays become public rituals, whose beliefs are treated as traditional, and whose objections are dismissed as threats. The result is a political culture in which Christianity can be simultaneously described as persecuted and dominant, silenced and amplified, under attack and legislatively powerful. It is a contradiction with excellent public relations.

This article argues that Christianity becomes most dangerous in public life when it is transformed from a source of personal conviction into a claim of political authority. Its influence is not inherently harmful; indeed, Christianity has contributed to movements for justice, compassion, and human dignity. But when political leaders use Christian identity performatively, when religious movements seek to impose doctrine through law, when national belonging is measured by proximity to a dominant faith, and when theology is allowed to restrict public inquiry, Christianity becomes a tool of control rather than conscience. The danger is not faith speaking in public. The danger is faith ruling by force.

The broader concern extends beyond Christianity. Any religion can become coercive when fused with state power. Any tradition can be used to define insiders and outsiders, sanctify authority, punish dissent, or limit knowledge. Christianity is the focus here because of its particular influence on American political life and its continuing role in debates over law, rights, science, medicine, and national identity. But the underlying question is larger: how can a pluralistic society protect religious freedom without allowing religious authority to dominate public life?

A democratic society does not require the absence of religion. It requires limits on what religion may do through the state. Faith may guide individuals. It may shape communities. It may inspire moral debate, social reform, ethical reflection, and acts of courage. But it should not determine the rights, bodies, knowledge, or futures of those who do not belong to it. When faith becomes law, it risks losing its soul while taking freedom with it. The task, then, is not to drive Christianity out of public life, but to prevent any faith from becoming a government weapon. In a society that claims to value both liberty and belief, that boundary is not hostility toward religion. It is the condition that allows freedom of conscience to survive.


# Christianity as a Cultural and Moral Force

Before Christianity can be criticized as a vehicle of political control, it must first be understood as a genuine cultural and moral force. Its influence on American life is not imaginary, incidental, or merely symbolic. Christianity helped shape the language of morality, obligation, charity, justice, family, suffering, forgiveness, reform, and public duty. It provided many Americans with a vocabulary for interpreting both private life and public responsibility. For generations, Christian belief did not remain confined to Sunday worship; it moved through schools, voluntary associations, reform movements, hospitals, abolitionist arguments, civil rights organizing, and the everyday rituals of civic identity. That influence is historically undeniable, even when its consequences are deeply contested.

In colonial America, Christianity was woven directly into the formation of many communities. The Library of Congress notes that several colonies, including New England colonies, New Jersey, Pennsylvania, and Maryland, were conceived and established as “plantations of religion,” even though settlers also came for economic, political, and practical reasons [@loc_religion_colonial]. Religion shaped local law, education, communal discipline, and ideas of public order. It also shaped how early Americans imagined moral citizenship. To live in community was often to live under shared religious assumptions, whether one accepted them freely or inherited them as part of the cultural air. Christianity was not simply one belief among many; in many places, it functioned as the background music of public life. Sometimes uplifting, sometimes overplayed, and occasionally blasted at full volume by people who had lost the plot.

That influence continued into the founding era, although it existed alongside a developing concern about religious liberty and governmental limits. The Continental-Confederation Congress included many deeply religious men, and the Library of Congress observes that Congress invested considerable energy in encouraging religious practice in the new nation [@loc_religion_congress]. At the same time, the constitutional framework eventually placed limits on formal religious establishment. The First Amendment, ratified in 1791, forbade Congress from making any law “respecting an establishment of religion,” while also protecting free exercise [@loc_religion_first_amendment; @archives_bill_of_rights]. This tension is central to American religious history: Christianity influenced public life, but the constitutional order resisted making one church or doctrine the legal foundation of the state. From the beginning, the United States lived with a contradiction it has never fully resolved: culturally Christian in many of its assumptions, yet legally committed to religious liberty.

Christianity’s cultural power was not only institutional; it was also ethical. Christian teachings about charity, mercy, neighborly obligation, and care for the vulnerable helped shape forms of social service and communal responsibility. Histories of medicine and nursing have long noted the role of Christian charity in the development of hospitals and care for the sick. A historical review indexed by PubMed describes the origins of the public hospital as emerging in the early Christian period, when Christian teaching encouraged assistance to the sick and poor [@pubmed_public_hospital_origins]. The University of Pennsylvania’s nursing history project similarly notes that early Christian benevolent outreach included caring for the sick, feeding the hungry, caring for widows and children, clothing the poor, and offering hospitality to strangers; that religious ethos continued through monastic institutions in the fifth and sixth centuries and into the Middle Ages [@upenn_history_hospitals]. These traditions did not produce modern medicine by themselves, and it would be simplistic to pretend they did. But they helped create durable expectations that care for the sick and poor was not merely private sentiment; it was a moral duty.

This charitable legacy matters because it reveals Christianity at its best: not as a force that controls outsiders, but as a force that binds communities to the needs of the vulnerable. In this form, Christian moral language can expand responsibility rather than narrow freedom. It asks not only what individuals are allowed to do, but what they owe to one another. It can challenge indifference, expose cruelty, and insist that human suffering is not someone else’s inconvenience. That is no small contribution. A society without a moral language of care becomes efficient, perhaps, but also colder than a hospital waiting room at 3 a.m. under fluorescent lights. No one needs that level of spiritual refrigeration.

Christianity also played an important role in reform movements, including movements that challenged the very injustices other Christians defended. This contradiction is especially clear in the history of slavery and abolition. Christian arguments were used both to justify slavery and to condemn it, which is one of the great moral fractures in American religious history. The Library of Congress documents the role of Quakers and other religious groups in antislavery work, noting that Quaker petitions on behalf of emancipation flowed into colonial legislatures and later to the United States Congress [@loc_abolition_quakers]. Another Library of Congress exhibition states that Quakers, most Black Christians, and other religious groups argued that slavery was incompatible with Christ’s teaching [@loc_africanamerican_abolition]. These arguments did not erase Christianity’s complicity in slavery, but they do show that Christian moral reasoning also became a weapon against human bondage.

The same pattern appears in the modern civil rights movement. Black churches were not merely spiritual spaces; they were organizing spaces, educational spaces, fundraising spaces, emotional refuges, and centers of political strategy. The Southern Christian Leadership Conference was founded in 1957 as an offshoot of the Montgomery Improvement Association after the Montgomery bus boycott, with Martin Luther King Jr., Ralph Abernathy, Fred Shuttlesworth, Bayard Rustin, and others helping create a regional organization to coordinate civil rights protest activity across the South [@nps_sclc]. The King Institute notes that through its church affiliations and advocacy of nonviolence, the SCLC sought to frame the struggle for civil rights in moral terms [@kinginstitute_sclc]. This was Christianity functioning not as state power, but as resistance to state-sanctioned injustice. It gave religious language to a political demand: that dignity, equality, and freedom were not privileges granted by the powerful, but moral claims rooted in the worth of human beings.

This distinction is essential. Christianity’s influence has been most defensible when it has expanded the circle of concern, challenged cruelty, and called power to account. It has been most dangerous when it has narrowed the circle, defended hierarchy, and demanded state enforcement of its own moral boundaries. The same tradition can produce both. That is why the question cannot be whether Christianity has shaped public life; clearly, it has. The more important question is what kind of shaping is taking place. Is faith being used to feed, heal, liberate, and protect? Or is it being used to police, punish, exclude, and dominate?

A serious analysis must hold both realities at once. Christianity has inspired compassion and control, liberation and domination, reform and repression. It has helped build hospitals and justify hierarchies. It has nourished abolitionist conviction and supplied language for segregationist resistance. It has strengthened civil rights movements and energized movements that seek to restrict the rights of others. The historical record does not allow for a clean hero or villain narrative. Christianity is not a single political actor with a single moral consequence. It is a vast and internally contested tradition whose public meaning depends on who uses it, toward what end, and with what relationship to power.

This is why the central concern of this article is not Christianity’s presence in society. A pluralistic democracy has room for religious belief, religious speech, religious organizing, and religious moral argument. The concern begins when Christian moral influence is converted into legal authority over people who do not share the faith or its interpretation. Christianity as a cultural and moral force can call people toward responsibility, compassion, and justice. Christianity as political authority can compel obedience, enforce conformity, and punish dissent. The difference between those two roles is the difference between conscience and control. And that difference is where the rest of this article must turn.


# From Moral Persuasion to Legal Control

The difference between moral persuasion and legal control is the difference between asking and compelling. Religious belief can shape how people understand justice, responsibility, family, sexuality, death, suffering, and the purpose of human life. It can lead people to vote, speak, organize, protest, donate, serve, and advocate. In a democracy, that participation is not only permitted; it is expected. Citizens do not enter public life as blank slates, scrubbed clean of conscience like someone preparing for airport security. They bring their values with them, including religious values. The problem begins when one religious tradition moves beyond persuasion and seeks the power of law to bind everyone, including those who do not share its premises.

This distinction sits at the center of the American constitutional experiment. The First Amendment protects both sides of religious liberty: it prohibits Congress from establishing religion while also protecting the free exercise of religion. Its language is brief but radical: “Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof” [@archives_bill_of_rights]. That pairing matters. The state may not create an official religion, but it also may not suppress religious practice simply because it is religious. Religious freedom, in this structure, is not achieved by making faith disappear from public life. It is achieved by limiting the government’s ability to privilege, punish, or enforce religion.

That balance has always been difficult because religion is not merely private sentiment. It becomes culture, law, custom, identity, and political language. Christianity, in particular, has often operated in the United States as both a faith tradition and a cultural default. This means Christian moral assumptions can appear neutral simply because they are familiar. A law shaped by Christian ideas about marriage, sexuality, reproduction, education, or family may be presented as “traditional values” rather than religious enforcement. That is part of the problem. When the dominant faith speaks through law, it often does not hear itself as sectarian. It hears itself as common sense. Power has many talents; self-awareness is rarely one of them.

The law’s power is different from the church’s power because law carries the force of the state. A church may teach that a behavior is sinful. A law may punish it. A religious community may define marriage, gender, sexuality, or family according to its doctrine. A government may restrict recognition, access, rights, funding, speech, education, or bodily autonomy. Once religious morality becomes legal control, dissent is no longer merely theological disagreement. It becomes vulnerability before the state. The question shifts from “What does this community believe?” to “What must everyone obey?”

This is why church-state disputes often emerge most intensely in public institutions, especially schools. Public schools are not churches, yet they are places where children encounter civic authority before they fully understand it. In *Engel v. Vitale*, the Supreme Court held that a public school system’s use of an official prayer violated the Establishment Clause [@oyez_engel_v_vitale]. The issue was not whether prayer itself was forbidden, but whether government officials could compose and facilitate prayer in public schools. The decision recognized that even a voluntary prayer can carry the pressure of official approval when placed inside a state-run institution. Children are very good at noticing what adults reward, even when adults pretend the room is neutral. That is not theology; that is classroom survival.

Later Establishment Clause cases tried to define when government action improperly advanced religion. In *Lemon v. Kurtzman*, the Supreme Court developed what became known as the Lemon test, requiring that a law have a secular legislative purpose, not primarily advance or inhibit religion, and not create excessive government entanglement with religion [@oyez_lemon_v_kurtzman]. Whatever one thinks of the test or its later decline, its underlying anxiety was clear: when government funds, endorses, or coordinates with religious institutions, the boundary between public authority and religious authority becomes unstable. The question is not only whether the state is hostile to religion. It is also whether the state is lending religion its badge, budget, or coercive force.

More recent cases show how difficult that boundary remains. In *Kennedy v. Bremerton School District*, the Supreme Court held that a public school district violated a football coach’s First Amendment rights when it disciplined him for postgame prayer, with the Court characterizing the prayer as private religious expression rather than government speech [@oyez_kennedy_bremerton]. The case reflects the modern tension clearly: protecting religious expression can be necessary to religious liberty, yet public religious expression by authority figures may also create pressure on those under their supervision. The difficult question is not whether the coach has religious rights. He does. The question is how those rights operate in a public-school environment where students may reasonably perceive participation, approval, or silence as socially required. Teenagers can detect pressure from three hallways away; pretending otherwise is adorable, but not persuasive.

The same tension appears in public funding. In *Carson v. Makin*, the Supreme Court held that Maine violated the Free Exercise Clause when it excluded certain religious schools from a tuition assistance program that otherwise supported private education [@scotus_carson_makin]. Supporters viewed the decision as protecting religious institutions from unequal treatment. Critics worried that it blurred the line between public funds and religious instruction. Both concerns reveal the same constitutional dilemma: the state may not discriminate against religion simply because it is religious, but public funding of religious institutions raises serious questions about whether taxpayers are being made to support doctrine they do not share. The conflict is not simple, which is deeply inconvenient for everyone who wanted a slogan instead of a constitutional crisis.

The movement from moral persuasion to legal control is especially visible in debates over reproduction. Abortion law has long been shaped by competing claims about privacy, bodily autonomy, fetal life, religious doctrine, medical ethics, and state power. In *Dobbs v. Jackson Women’s Health Organization*, the Supreme Court upheld Mississippi’s 15-week abortion ban and overturned *Roe v. Wade* and *Planned Parenthood v. Casey*, returning abortion regulation to elected representatives and the states [@scotus_dobbs_jackson]. The decision itself was framed in constitutional terms, not as a theological ruling. Yet the political movements that helped produce the post-*Roe* landscape have been deeply shaped by religious claims about life, personhood, sexuality, and women’s roles. This is where the distinction between religious moral argument and legal control becomes urgent. A religious community may believe abortion is morally wrong. But when that belief becomes law, it governs people who may not share the same theology, moral anthropology, or interpretation of when personhood begins.

This does not mean religious people should be excluded from abortion debates or any other moral debate. Such exclusion would itself violate the pluralism this article defends. Religious citizens have the right to argue that law should protect unborn life. Secular citizens have the right to argue that law should protect bodily autonomy, reproductive freedom, and medical decision-making. The democratic problem arises when one side’s religious premise becomes the state’s coercive rule without adequate regard for those outside that belief system. At that point, religious conviction no longer functions only as conscience. It becomes governance over other people’s bodies.

The same pattern appears in debates over sexuality, gender, education, and family structure. Political Christianity often frames these issues as moral emergencies requiring legal intervention. The language may be protection: protecting children, protecting families, protecting tradition, protecting religious liberty. Sometimes protection is sincere. But protection can also become a polished word for control. It can justify book bans, restrictions on classroom discussion, limits on gender expression, barriers to LGBTQ recognition, and policies that treat certain lives as threats to public order. When religious morality enters law through the language of protection, the question must always be asked: who is being protected, from whom, and at what cost?

This is not merely a historical concern or a theoretical warning. The struggle over religious authority is unfolding in current law and policy. Public-school religion disputes continue through efforts to place the Ten Commandments, biblical material, or religiously framed moral instruction inside state institutions [@ap_texas_ten_commandments_2026; @ap_louisiana_ten_commandments_2026]. Abortion restrictions after *Dobbs* continue to reshape bodily autonomy across the country, with some states banning abortion entirely and others imposing gestational limits [@guttmacher_abortion_bans_2026]. LGBTQ-related school policies, curriculum restrictions, and gender-related laws likewise show how claims of moral protection can become mechanisms of exclusion [@map_lgbtq_curricular_laws]. These developments should raise alarm precisely because they demonstrate that the movement from persuasion to control is not a relic of the past. It is happening now, in classrooms, clinics, courts, legislatures, and families. The question is no longer whether faith can become law. The question is how much of public life will be handed over before people recognize the cost.

The danger becomes sharper when political Christianity treats compromise itself as moral surrender. In a pluralistic democracy, no group gets everything it wants, because everyone must share public life with people who believe differently. Yet politicized religious movements often demand maximum accommodation for themselves while resisting accommodation for others. Their position becomes less about coexistence and more about ownership: public life must affirm their beliefs, public institutions must reflect their morality, and dissenting citizens must accept limits framed as religious freedom. This is not genuine pluralism. It is domination speaking the language of liberty.

Legal control also changes the emotional experience of citizenship. A person may disagree with a church and simply leave that church. They cannot simply leave the law. When Christian doctrine shapes policy, those outside the doctrine may feel that their government has become an extension of someone else’s pulpit. They may experience public institutions not as neutral guardians of equal citizenship, but as moral enforcers of a faith they never joined. That is the difference between influence and domination. Influence invites argument. Domination removes the exit.

The danger is not that Christianity has moral opinions. Every tradition has moral opinions. The danger is that Christianity, because of its cultural dominance in the United States, can sometimes disguise sectarian claims as universal truths. A Christian argument against divorce, abortion, same-sex marriage, gender transition, or sexual freedom may be meaningful within Christian ethical life. But when such arguments are converted directly into civil law, they often demand obedience from people whose moral worlds are different. That is not religious freedom for all. It is religious authority for some, enforced by the state on everyone else.

This distinction also helps clarify why accusations of “hostility to religion” so often miss the point. To oppose the legal enforcement of a religious doctrine is not necessarily to oppose the religion itself. A person can respect Christianity and still reject Christian control over civil law. A person can defend religious liberty and still oppose government-led prayer, public funding of doctrinal instruction, or laws rooted primarily in sectarian morality. In fact, limiting the state’s religious power may be one of the clearest ways to protect religion from political corruption. Once faith gains access to state enforcement, it may win policy battles while losing moral credibility. Nothing says “spiritual depth” like needing a legislature to make your theology stick. Very subtle. Very Sermon on the Mount with a subpoena.

The movement from persuasion to control is therefore the central warning of this article. Christianity can shape conscience, inspire reform, and contribute to public moral debate. But when it becomes law, it enters a different moral category. It no longer merely asks what believers should do; it determines what everyone must endure. The question is not whether Christian arguments belong in democracy. They do. The question is whether Christian authority should govern those who do not consent to it. In a pluralistic society, the answer must be no. Faith may speak, plead, preach, organize, and persuade. But when faith becomes law, it must be judged not only by the sincerity of its believers, but by the freedom of those who are forced to live under it.


# When Doctrine Polices Knowledge

The struggle over religious authority does not stop at law, morality, bodies, or citizenship. It also reaches into knowledge itself. When religious doctrine claims the power to decide which questions may be asked, which discoveries may be trusted, which books may be read, which science may be taught, and which forms of research may be morally permitted, faith moves beyond conscience and becomes a gatekeeper of inquiry. This is one of the most dangerous forms of religious power because it does not merely tell people how to live. It attempts to decide what society is allowed to know.

The Galileo affair remains one of the clearest historical warnings. Galileo’s support for heliocentrism did not become controversial simply because it was an astronomical claim. It became dangerous because it collided with church authority, scriptural interpretation, and institutional control over truth. Galileo’s case did not emerge from nowhere; in 1616, the Sacred Congregation of the Index had already condemned Copernicus’s *De revolutionibus* pending correction, making clear that the conflict was not only about Galileo’s personality or tone, but about institutional resistance to a cosmology that unsettled theological authority [@stanford_galileo]. In 1633, Galileo was convicted of “vehement suspicion of heresy,” and his sentence of imprisonment was commuted to house arrest [@stanford_galileo]. The telescope had done something doctrine found inconvenient: it made the old certainty wobble. And few institutions respond gracefully when their certainty starts wobbling in public.

The point is not that Christianity is inherently opposed to science. That claim is too simple, and worse, too easy to dismiss. Christian institutions have supported education, medicine, astronomy, philosophy, and scientific inquiry in many contexts. The Vatican itself later reflected on the Galileo case, and John Paul II’s 1992 address emphasized that theologians should remain informed about scientific advances [@pas_jpii_galileo_1992]. That acknowledgment matters because it reveals the deeper issue: the danger was never curiosity itself. The danger was institutional authority trying to control the terms under which curiosity could speak.

Galileo’s case was not an isolated moment of religious anxiety over knowledge. It belonged to a broader tradition of institutional censorship that treated certain ideas as dangerous not always because they were false, but because they threatened theological control. The Catholic Church’s *Index Librorum Prohibitorum* functioned for centuries as an official mechanism for identifying prohibited books, and the Vatican’s 1966 notification abolishing its legal force still described the Index as a tool historically used to safeguard faith and morals [@vatican_index_1966]. In that context, the issue was not only whether one astronomer could defend heliocentrism. It was whether religious authority could define the boundaries of permissible reading, teaching, and intellectual life. Very trusting of the faithful, apparently, as long as no one read without supervision.

Galileo’s case is powerful because it shows what happens when evidence becomes threatening not because it is weak, but because it is disruptive. The church did not merely disagree with a scientific claim; it disciplined the person advancing it. That distinction matters. Disagreement belongs to inquiry. Discipline belongs to power. A religious institution may interpret the world through theology, but when it punishes inquiry because inquiry unsettles doctrine, it crosses from belief into control. It no longer asks whether a claim is true. It asks whether truth has permission.

The same pattern appears in American fights over evolution education. In 1925, Tennessee’s Butler Act prohibited the teaching of evolution in public educational institutions, leading to the prosecution of John Scopes [@tennessee_butler_act_1925; @aclu_scopes_trial]. The issue was not simply whether one teacher could discuss Darwin. It was whether religious objections to evolution could determine the boundaries of public science education. Decades later, the Supreme Court struck down an Arkansas anti-evolution law in *Epperson v. Arkansas*, holding that the state could not forbid teaching human evolution in public schools to protect a particular religious view [@oyez_epperson_arkansas]. When outright bans became constitutionally indefensible, the strategy shifted. In *Edwards v. Aguillard*, the Court rejected Louisiana’s requirement that “creation science” be taught alongside evolution, and in *Kitzmiller v. Dover*, a federal court rejected intelligent design as a religious view rather than a scientific theory [@oyez_edwards_aguillard; @kitzmiller_dover_decision]. The pattern is difficult to miss: when doctrine could not openly ban science, it attempted to rename itself science and enter the classroom through the side door. Very discreet, like a rhinestone cross on a bulldozer.

This pattern also appears in efforts to control reproductive and sexual knowledge. The Comstock Act treated materials related to contraception and abortion as obscene and restricted their circulation through the mail, including writings and instruments connected to contraception and abortion even when connected to medical information [@mtsu_comstock_act; @house_comstock_records]. In this case, moral regulation did not only target sexual behavior. It targeted the circulation of knowledge itself.

Later debates over abstinence-only sex education repeated the logic in another form: rather than equip young people with comprehensive, medically accurate information, public policy often elevated moral instruction over evidence. The failure becomes even clearer when abstinence-only education is judged by its own stated goal. If the purpose is to reduce teen pregnancy, delay sexual activity, and protect young people, the evidence is not flattering. A state-level study found that greater emphasis on abstinence-only education was associated with higher teen pregnancy and birth rates, while comprehensive sex and STI education correlated with the lowest teen pregnancy rates [@stanger_hall_abstinence_2011]. A later review in the *Journal of Adolescent Health* concluded that abstinence-only-until-marriage programs are not effective and may withhold or distort medically relevant information [@santelli_abstinence_only_2017].

The contrast between evidence-based access and moral restriction is visible in policy outcomes as well. Colorado’s Family Planning Initiative expanded access to long-acting reversible contraception and reported that teen birth and teen abortion rates were nearly cut in half [@colorado_larc_success]. Texas, by contrast, had a large share of districts teaching abstinence-only or no sex education, and research on Texas family-planning cuts found that reduced access increased teen birth rates by approximately 3.4 percent over four years [@tfn_texas_sex_ed_2017; @packham_family_planning_2017]. The lesson is blunt: withholding information does not make young people safer. It only makes ignorance feel morally supervised.

That question has not disappeared. Modern debates over stem-cell research reveal a related pattern, though the details require care. Stem-cell research is not a single category. The National Institutes of Health distinguishes among embryonic stem cells, induced pluripotent stem cells, and adult or somatic stem cells [@nih_stem_cell_basics]. That distinction matters because religious opposition, especially in Catholic teaching, has focused most strongly on embryonic stem-cell research because of claims about embryos, personhood, and the beginning of human life [@vatican_dignitas_personae]. Other forms of stem-cell research have often been treated differently.

This nuance is important because the argument is not that religious ethics should be expelled from scientific debate. Ethical reflection is necessary, especially in research involving embryos, reproduction, disability, consent, genetic intervention, medical access, and human vulnerability. Science can tell society what may be possible, but it cannot by itself answer every question about what should be done. The problem begins when one religious doctrine becomes the controlling boundary for public research, public funding, and medical possibility. Ethical debate is one thing. Theological veto power over a pluralistic society is another.

Stem-cell politics shows how doctrine can move from belief into policy. A religious community may sincerely believe that embryonic research violates the dignity of human life. Within that community, such a belief may guide moral practice. But when that belief shapes public funding restrictions, research access, or medical policy for people who do not share the same theological premises, the issue becomes larger than religious conscience. It becomes public control. The question is no longer, “What does this faith tradition teach its believers?” The question becomes, “Whose doctrine is allowed to limit everyone’s future?”

That future is not abstract. Medical research is not a luxury item sitting behind glass for philosophers to admire. It affects patients, families, physicians, scientists, disabled people, people with degenerative diseases, and people waiting for treatments that may or may not arrive in time. When doctrine narrows the scope of inquiry, the cost is not only intellectual. It is bodily. It is emotional. It is measured in waiting rooms, failed treatments, lost time, and families trying to survive uncertainty with less help than science might have been allowed to offer. The moral stakes are not only in the embryo, as religious arguments often insist. They are also in the living people whose suffering is prolonged when research is politically trapped.

This is where the pattern becomes familiar. Religious authority often presents itself as protecting life, truth, morality, children, or human dignity. Sometimes it is sincere. Sometimes it raises necessary ethical questions. But when protection becomes prohibition, and prohibition becomes policy, the protective language begins to do another kind of work. It shields control from scrutiny. It frames scientific possibility as moral threat. It treats inquiry as rebellion. And suddenly the people asking questions are not researchers, physicians, patients, teachers, students, or ethicists; they are dangers to the moral order. Very convenient. Nothing says confidence in truth like being terrified of investigation.

These examples are not identical. Galileo concerns astronomy and scriptural interpretation. The Index concerns reading and intellectual control. Evolution disputes concern science education. Comstock and abstinence-only policy concern reproductive and sexual knowledge. Stem-cell debates concern bioethics, embryos, medicine, and public funding. But they reveal a shared danger: doctrine policing the boundary of permissible knowledge. In each case, religious authority does not merely participate in debate. It attempts to define the limits of what can be read, taught, funded, researched, discussed, or pursued. That is a profound form of power because whoever controls inquiry also controls possibility.

This matters for the article’s broader argument because law is not the only way faith becomes coercive. Faith can become law through legislation, but it can also become law through funding rules, institutional pressure, school policy, medical restriction, censorship, and public fear. It can control not only what people do, but what they are allowed to learn, test, imagine, and heal. That is why religious authority over knowledge should alarm anyone who cares about both democracy and faith. A pluralistic society cannot allow one doctrine to become the ceiling of human inquiry.

The question, then, is not whether religion may ask moral questions of science. It should. The question is whether religious authority should be allowed to close the door before the questions can even be examined. Science without ethics can become reckless, but ethics without humility can become tyranny in a lab coat. The task is not to silence faith, but to prevent doctrine from becoming a lock on the future. When faith polices knowledge, it does more than defend belief. It teaches society to fear discovery. And a society that fears discovery has already begun surrendering its freedom.


# Selective Literalism and Moral Convenience

Selective literalism is not simply a way of reading scripture. It is a way of deciding when mercy is allowed and when control is required. In politicized Christianity, some biblical teachings are treated as timeless, rigid, and ready for civil law, while others are softened through context, pastoral care, historical distance, or quiet avoidance. That does not mean interpretation itself is dishonest. Every living religious tradition interprets. The problem begins when a movement denies that it is interpreting at all, then uses its selected readings to govern people outside the faith. At that point, interpretation stops looking like humility before scripture and starts looking like power choosing its favorite verses.

The history of slavery and abolition reveals how morally consequential selective interpretation can become. Christianity in the United States was not simply “for” or “against” slavery in any clean or unified way. Christian arguments were used on both sides. Proslavery advocates, including clergy, appealed to biblical references to defend bondage and present slavery as compatible with Christian order [@nhc_american_abolition_religion]. At the same time, abolitionists used Christian moral language to condemn slavery as a violation of human dignity, divine justice, and the teachings of Christ. The Library of Congress notes that Quakers and other religious groups organized antislavery petitions and abolitionist work, while many Black Christians and other religious communities argued that slavery was incompatible with Christ’s teaching [@loc_abolition_quakers; @loc_africanamerican_abolition]. The same religious tradition was being pulled in opposite moral directions.

That contradiction matters because it shows that appeals to “biblical authority” do not interpret themselves. People choose which texts to emphasize, which contexts to invoke, which moral themes to elevate, and which human beings to center. Enslavers and their defenders could point to biblical references to slavery and use them as permission structures for domination. Abolitionists could point to liberation, justice, human dignity, and the moral scandal of oppression. Both sides claimed religious seriousness, but they did not produce morally equal outcomes. One used faith to defend ownership of human beings. The other used faith to demand their freedom. If that does not expose the danger of selective interpretation, the siren is apparently broken.

The slavery example also shows why “the Bible says” is not enough as a public argument. A text may be cited with confidence while being used to protect cruelty. A religious claim may sound traditional while serving power. A community may insist it is simply obeying scripture while conveniently reading scripture in ways that preserve its wealth, hierarchy, and social control. This is the danger of selective literalism: it can make oppression appear obedient and make liberation appear rebellious. History has already shown where that road can lead, and it was not a scenic route.

This problem becomes sharper because “the Bible” is not one unmediated English document that dropped whole and footnoted from the sky. The writings that now make up the Bible passed through languages, manuscripts, translations, communities, theological debates, and canon formation. The New Testament writings emerged decades after the life of Jesus, while the recognition of the New Testament canon took shape over several centuries [@yale_nt_canon; @byu_nt_canonization]. Other early Christian writings circulated outside what later became the canon, and not every ancient Christian community agreed immediately on which texts should be treated as authoritative [@byu_nt_canonization]. None of this means scripture has no meaning. It means claims of “plain literal reading” are often far less plain than their defenders pretend.

Translation deepens the issue. Modern readers usually encounter scripture through English versions that make interpretive choices about ancient Hebrew, Aramaic, and Greek. Words do not move across languages like sealed containers. They carry context, ambiguity, theological tradition, and the assumptions of translators. That does not make scripture useless or meaningless; it makes humility necessary. If a text reaches modern readers through history, language, transmission, selection, and interpretation, then using one preferred English reading as a weapon of law should make everyone nervous. The problem is not that people interpret scripture. The problem is pretending they do not, especially when the interpretation comes with handcuffs attached.

Divorce is one of the most revealing examples. The New Testament contains strict teachings on divorce and remarriage. In Matthew, Jesus teaches that divorce and remarriage can be connected to adultery, with an exception related to sexual immorality [@bible_matthew_19_9]. Mark presents the teaching even more starkly, describing remarriage after divorce as adultery for both men and women [@bible_mark_10_11_12]. These are not obscure verses hiding in a biblical attic under a box labeled “things no one wants to discuss.” They are direct teachings attributed to Jesus.

Yet in much of modern American Christianity, divorce is treated with considerable pastoral flexibility. That flexibility may be humane, and often it should be. People leave marriages because of abuse, abandonment, betrayal, danger, emotional destruction, incompatibility, addiction, coercion, or simple human failure. A rigid rule that traps someone in harm would not be moral seriousness; it would be cruelty with a hymnal. Many churches recognize this, formally or informally. They may still teach that divorce is painful or morally serious, but they often make room for forgiveness, remarriage, restoration, and continued belonging.

That pastoral flexibility is not the issue. In fact, it can reflect Christianity at its most compassionate: a recognition that human life is complicated, suffering is real, and moral judgment without mercy becomes its own form of violence. The problem is that the same interpretive generosity is not consistently extended to sexuality, gender identity, reproductive autonomy, or nontraditional family structures. When the subject is divorce, many communities discover context, complexity, healing, and grace. When the subject is LGBTQ people, abortion, contraception, gender transition, or queer families, some of those same movements suddenly become allergic to nuance. Apparently context is available by appointment only.

This is the standard that should apply consistently: if modern perspective is allowed for one teaching, it should be allowed for all. If historical context, pastoral concern, social change, trauma, abuse, economic reality, translation, canon formation, and human suffering can shape how Christians interpret divorce, slavery, wealth, or violence, then those same tools cannot suddenly be forbidden when the subject becomes sexuality, gender, reproduction, or family structure. A tradition may decide where it lands morally, but it should be honest about the process. Selective literalism becomes dishonest when modern interpretation is used to protect the familiar and then denied to those whose lives make the dominant group uncomfortable.

Reproductive politics exposes this selectivity with particular force. Modern anti-abortion politics often treats “life begins at conception” as if it were the only possible biblical position, but the scriptural and interpretive record is more complicated than that. Genesis describes the first human becoming a living being when God breathes the breath of life into his nostrils [@bible_genesis_2_7]. Some Jewish interpretations have therefore connected life and legal personhood more closely to breath, birth, and independent life than to conception alone [@thetorah_life_begins_breath]. Jewish legal interpretation has also often treated the fetus as not yet a full legal person until birth, with Exodus 21:22–23 interpreted by many traditions as requiring financial damages for fetal loss rather than treating it as homicide [@myjewishlearning_fetus_jewish_law].

That does not settle every ethical question about abortion, and it should not be flattened into a slogan from the other side either. But it does expose the problem: a contested theological claim about when personhood begins is often presented as if scripture speaks with one simple voice, then converted into law for people who do not share that interpretation. If religious movements are willing to acknowledge complexity around divorce, slavery, wealth, and historical context, then reproductive ethics cannot honestly be treated as the one area where interpretation suddenly vanishes and only enforcement remains.

This selective pattern matters politically because divorce, despite strict biblical language, is not usually treated by Christian political movements as a major target for civil prohibition. There is no broad national movement to ban remarriage after divorce, deny public benefits to divorced people, censor classroom discussion of divorced families, or prevent divorced people from holding public office. Divorced Christians are not typically treated as existential threats to children, civilization, or the moral survival of the nation. They may be judged socially in some communities, but they are not usually made the central object of legislative panic. That difference is revealing.

The difference cannot be explained simply by saying that Christians care about scripture. If biblical authority alone determined political urgency, divorce would occupy a far more central place in Christian political activism. Instead, the verses on divorce are often managed through pastoral discretion, while verses or traditions invoked against LGBTQ people and reproductive freedom are treated as if they demand public enforcement. This is where selective literalism becomes moral convenience. The text is strict when strictness controls the vulnerable. The text becomes flexible when flexibility protects the familiar.

Wealth provides another example. The New Testament contains repeated warnings about money, greed, and indifference to the poor. Jesus warns that one cannot serve both God and wealth [@bible_matthew_6_24]. In the story of the rich ruler, Jesus tells a wealthy man to sell what he owns, distribute the money to the poor, and follow him; when the man refuses, Jesus describes the difficulty of the rich entering the kingdom of God [@bible_luke_18_22_25]. Matthew 25 frames care for the hungry, thirsty, stranger, naked, sick, and imprisoned as morally inseparable from service to Christ [@bible_matthew_25_35_40]. These teachings are not vague decorative embroidery around the edges of Christianity. They are central to the ethical imagination of the New Testament.

And yet wealth is rarely treated by politicized Christianity with the same legal urgency applied to sexuality and reproduction. There is no comparable movement to criminalize greed, legally limit luxury, restructure economic life around Jesus’s warnings about wealth, or deny political legitimacy to leaders whose policies harm the poor. Politicians may invoke Christianity while supporting cuts to social support, hostility toward migrants, punitive poverty policy, or economic systems that reward extraction and indifference. The contradiction is not subtle. A politics that quotes scripture to regulate bodies but misplaces the Bible when the poor need food has not discovered moral clarity. It has discovered convenience.

This is not to say that Christian communities never care for the poor. Many do, deeply and sacrificially. Churches feed people, shelter people, visit prisoners, support refugees, fund medical care, run charities, and build networks of survival where the state fails. That work matters, and it should not be dismissed. The issue is the gap between Christian service and Christian politics. A church may feed the hungry on Saturday while supporting policies on Tuesday that make hunger more likely. A movement may praise charity while opposing structural responsibility. It may call poverty a spiritual concern but not a legislative emergency. The result is a moral split: mercy as private virtue, punishment as public policy.

That split is central to selective literalism. Scripture is treated as binding when it can be used to discipline outsiders, but negotiable when it would challenge insiders. Sexual minorities, women seeking reproductive care, trans people, unmarried people, students, and families outside conservative norms are often told that biblical teaching leaves no room for compromise. But when scripture confronts slavery, wealth, exploitation, divorce, hypocrisy, violence, cruelty, or neglect of the vulnerable, suddenly interpretation gets very sophisticated. Hebrew and Greek are examined. Historical context is invoked. Pastoral discretion appears. Complexity enters the sanctuary wearing sensible shoes.

This double standard reveals that the issue is not merely belief. It is power. Religious movements tend to enforce most aggressively those teachings that preserve hierarchy, identity, and cultural control. Teachings that would require the dominant group to sacrifice comfort, wealth, status, or political advantage are more easily softened. This does not mean every believer is cynical. Many are sincere. But sincerity does not erase the pattern. A person may sincerely believe they are defending biblical truth while participating in a system that treats some biblical commands as urgent and others as optional.

Selective literalism also protects performative Christianity. It allows political leaders to claim religious authority without submitting to religious accountability. A politician can gesture toward Christianity by opposing abortion, attacking LGBTQ rights, endorsing school prayer, or invoking “family values,” while ignoring Christian teachings on humility, truthfulness, mercy, poverty, peacemaking, and care for the stranger. The performance works because the public checklist has already been narrowed. Christianity is reduced to a handful of identity markers, and moral seriousness becomes a branding exercise. Say the right things about sex and the rest of the Sermon on the Mount can apparently wait in the lobby.

The danger grows when selective interpretation becomes law. Within a religious community, believers may debate doctrine, discipline, forgiveness, and moral practice. But when selected doctrines become civil law, selective literalism no longer remains an internal religious problem. It becomes a public injustice. The state begins enforcing the parts of religion that political movements find useful while ignoring the parts that would restrain their own power. This is not faithfulness. It is editing with police power.

A pluralistic democracy cannot prevent religious people from interpreting scripture selectively. Every community makes interpretive choices. But democracy can refuse to let one group’s selective theology become everyone else’s legal burden. A religious movement may believe its interpretation is true. It may preach it, teach it, defend it, and organize around it. But when that interpretation is applied through law to people outside the faith, its selectivity must be scrutinized. The question is not only “What does this tradition teach?” The question is “Why is this teaching being enforced, while others are excused?”

Selective literalism is therefore not just a theological inconsistency. It is a political strategy, whether intentional or not. It allows religious authority to appear principled while functioning selectively. It turns interpretation into inevitability, preference into doctrine, and cultural discomfort into divine command. The result is moral convenience dressed as conviction, and the outfit is still not as flattering as it thinks.


# Performative Christianity and Political Legitimacy

Performative Christianity is not simply hypocrisy, although hypocrisy is certainly one of its more enthusiastic interns. It is the use of Christian language, symbols, and identity markers to claim moral legitimacy without submitting to the ethical demands those symbols are supposed to carry. In public life, Christianity can become less a faith tradition than a political costume: a way to signal belonging, reassure a base, attack opponents, and present power as righteousness. The performance works because Christian symbols still carry cultural authority in the United States. A Bible, a prayer, a church backdrop, a reference to “faith,” “family,” or “Judeo-Christian values” can make a political agenda appear morally serious before anyone has examined what that agenda actually does.

This is not the same as sincere religious expression. Many public officials are genuinely religious. Many voters are shaped by faith in honest, serious, and morally demanding ways. Religious language in politics is not automatically performative, and a democratic society should not treat it as suspect merely because it is religious. The problem begins when Christian identity becomes a substitute for Christian ethics. When the symbol matters more than the substance, when the language of faith is used to bless cruelty, and when religious affiliation becomes a loyalty badge rather than a moral discipline, Christianity is no longer guiding political life. It is being used as a prop.

That prop function is especially visible when politicians invoke Christianity while behaving in ways that would be difficult to reconcile with the ethical teachings they claim to defend. A leader may speak of faith while practicing dishonesty, contempt, vengeance, greed, scapegoating, or indifference to the vulnerable. A movement may describe itself as Christian while supporting policies that punish the poor, demonize migrants, mock weakness, excuse corruption, or treat cruelty as strength. In these cases, Christianity is not absent from politics; it is present as branding. The faith becomes a logo printed on a product that may contain none of the advertised ingredients.

One of the clearest modern examples of sacred symbolism as political theater was the Bible photo opportunity outside St. John’s Church in June 2020. Then-President Donald Trump stood outside the church holding a Bible after leaving the White House during nationwide protests following the murder of George Floyd [@ap_stjohns_photo_op_probe]. The image was powerful precisely because it was religiously staged: a president, a church, a Bible, cameras, and a national crisis. But the moral meaning of the symbol was deeply contested. The issue was not simply that a political leader held a Bible. The issue was that a sacred text appeared to be used as a visual instrument of authority, order, and domination rather than humility, repentance, or care for the wounded. It was less sermon than set design.

That episode matters because performative Christianity often depends on visual shorthand. It does not require careful theology. It requires recognizable signals. The Bible does not need to be opened. The prayer does not need to transform conduct. The church does not need to shape policy through mercy or justice. The symbol only needs to communicate alignment: this leader is on the side of “faith,” and therefore criticism can be framed as hostility to religion itself. The performance turns religious imagery into political armor. Once the armor is on, accountability becomes easier to deflect.

This is why the gap between religious image and religious conduct matters. When Christian language becomes a shield for political behavior, it can make moral criticism seem illegitimate. A leader can frame opposition as anti-Christian, anti-family, anti-tradition, or anti-American. A policy can be defended not because it is just, effective, or humane, but because it is said to represent religious values. In that environment, the content of the policy may matter less than the identity it performs. The question becomes not “Does this reflect mercy, justice, humility, truth, or care for the least of these?” but “Does this sound like it belongs to our side?” That is not moral reasoning. That is team merchandise with incense.

Performative Christianity also narrows the public definition of what counts as Christian. In American politics, Christianity is often reduced to a small set of symbolic issues: abortion, sexuality, gender, school prayer, religious displays, and opposition to secularism. These issues may matter deeply to many Christians, but they do not exhaust the tradition. A Christianity reduced to identity markers loses sight of teachings about poverty, truthfulness, peacemaking, forgiveness, humility, hospitality, greed, and care for strangers. It becomes possible for political leaders to appear “Christian” by performing the correct cultural signals while ignoring the harder ethical demands of the faith. The result is a version of Christianity that asks very little of power and very much of everyone power wants to control.

This narrowing is politically useful because it allows religious identity to be measured by affiliation rather than practice. A politician can be treated as a defender of Christianity even if many citizens do not view that politician as personally religious. Pew Research Center reported in 2026 that seven-in-ten Americans said Donald Trump was not too religious or not at all religious [@pew_trump_religiosity_2026]. That finding is revealing not because one politician’s personal faith can be measured perfectly from the outside, but because it shows how political religious legitimacy may not depend on perceived piety. A leader may be seen as religiously useful even if not seen as religiously devout. In performative politics, usefulness can matter more than holiness. The bar is not “Christlike.” The bar is “will fight for our side.” Conveniently low, but apparently very sturdy.

This dynamic does serious damage to democratic life. When Christianity is used as a legitimacy machine, political disagreement can be recoded as spiritual betrayal. Opponents are not merely wrong; they are godless, demonic, anti-family, anti-Christian, or enemies of the nation’s moral order. That shift raises the emotional stakes of politics and lowers the possibility of compromise. If one side is imagined as defending God and the other as opposing God, ordinary democratic negotiation starts to look like moral surrender. Debate becomes warfare. Policy becomes testimony. Elections become altar calls with better polling data and worse music.

Performative Christianity also damages Christianity itself. When religious symbols are repeatedly attached to cruelty, corruption, dishonesty, or domination, the public meaning of the faith changes. People outside the church may come to associate Christianity not with mercy, humility, forgiveness, or love of neighbor, but with control, grievance, exclusion, and political appetite. That is not persecution. That is brand damage, and the call may be coming from inside the sanctuary. A faith tradition can survive criticism from outsiders. It has a harder time surviving being reduced by its supposed defenders to a campaign aesthetic.

Performative Christianity does not merely misrepresent Christianity to outsiders; it can dilute Christianity for believers themselves. When faith is repeatedly reduced to political identity, culture-war signaling, and loyalty tests, the deeper demands of the tradition become easier to ignore. Mercy becomes optional. Humility becomes weakness. Care for the poor becomes charity branding rather than moral obligation. Truth becomes negotiable if the lie protects the movement. Repentance becomes unnecessary because the enemy is always worse. In that sense, performative Christianity does not defend Christianity so much as thin it out, stripping a demanding faith down into a set of slogans, symbols, and enemies.

The danger is especially sharp because performative Christianity often claims to be protecting religion. It presents itself as a defense of faith against secular hostility, moral decline, or cultural displacement. Sometimes those fears are sincerely felt. Many religious Americans do believe that religion’s influence is changing, and Pew has found that Americans remain divided over religion’s role in public life and government [@pew_religion_public_life_2026]. But fear of cultural change can make symbolic politics more attractive. When people feel their religious world slipping, a leader who performs strength on behalf of Christianity may feel like protection, even if the performance empties the faith of its ethical demands.

That is the central danger: performative Christianity can train people to confuse dominance with faithfulness. It suggests that Christianity is strongest when it wins elections, controls courts, commands schools, shapes law, and punishes dissent. But Christian moral credibility has rarely come from domination. It has come from witness, sacrifice, compassion, courage, and the willingness to stand with the vulnerable even when doing so costs something. When Christianity is performed mainly as power, it risks losing the very qualities that made it morally compelling in the first place.

A democratic society does not need public officials to hide their faith. It needs them to stop using faith as camouflage for power. Religious language may inspire moral seriousness, but it should also invite moral scrutiny. If a politician invokes Christianity, the fair question is not simply whether the words sound religious. The fair question is whether the politics reflect the ethical burden those words carry. Does the invocation of faith lead to mercy or punishment? Truth or manipulation? Humility or domination? Solidarity with the vulnerable or protection for the powerful?

Performative Christianity becomes dangerous when it turns those questions into disloyalty. It asks citizens to honor the symbol without examining the conduct. It treats religious language as proof of moral authority rather than a claim that must be tested. But faith that cannot survive scrutiny is not being protected; it is being managed. Christianity does not become stronger when politicians hold it up for cameras. It becomes stronger when its ethical demands are taken seriously, especially by those who claim it most loudly.

The issue, then, is not whether Christianity may appear in public life. It may. The issue is whether Christianity is being practiced as conscience or performed as legitimacy. A faith used to humble power is very different from a faith used to sanctify power. One can challenge injustice. The other can baptize it. Performative Christianity is dangerous because it allows political authority to borrow the language of holiness while avoiding the discipline of morality. It lets power dress itself in sacred symbols and then act offended when anyone notices the costume does not fit.


# Politicized Evangelicalism and Christian Nationalism

Politicized evangelicalism and Christian nationalism intensify the movement from faith as conscience to faith as political authority. They do not simply argue that Christians should participate in public life. That would be ordinary democratic participation. Instead, they often suggest that the nation itself is properly Christian, that Christian identity should shape public legitimacy, and that political disagreement with a conservative Christian agenda reflects moral, spiritual, or national decline. In this framework, Christianity is not merely a faith tradition. It becomes a claim about who the country belongs to.

This distinction matters because evangelical Christianity is not inherently nationalist, authoritarian, or politically coercive. Evangelical faith can be deeply personal, missionary, charitable, communal, and morally serious. It has also taken diverse political forms across history and across racial, regional, and denominational communities. The problem is not evangelical belief itself. The problem is politicized evangelicalism: the use of evangelical identity, language, networks, fears, and moral authority to build political power. That distinction is necessary because critique should be aimed at domination, not at sincere belief. The target is not Christians voting their conscience. The target is Christianity being turned into a citizenship test.

Christian nationalism goes further by tying Christianity to national identity. It suggests that the United States was founded as, or should become, a distinctly Christian nation whose laws, institutions, and public culture should reflect Christian authority. Pew Research Center found in 2022 that 45 percent of U.S. adults said the United States should be a “Christian nation,” although Americans meant different things by that phrase and many still supported church-state separation [@pew_christian_nation_2022]. That ambiguity matters. For some people, “Christian nation” may mean cultural heritage, moral aspiration, or recognition of Christianity’s historical influence. For others, it means a political order in which Christianity receives official privilege. The phrase sounds simple until someone asks who gets to define “Christian,” who gets protected by it, and who gets governed by it.

The constitutional problem is immediate. The First Amendment rejects religious establishment while protecting free exercise [@archives_bill_of_rights]. That means Christianity may influence citizens, communities, and moral debate, but the government may not become the instrument of one faith. Christian nationalism strains that boundary because it imagines national belonging through religious identity. It does not merely say Christians have rights within the nation. It implies that Christianity has a rightful claim over the nation. That is a very different proposition. One protects conscience. The other assigns ownership.

This ownership logic is why Christian nationalism is dangerous to pluralism. A pluralistic democracy assumes that people of many religions and no religion belong equally to the political community. Christian nationalism unsettles that equality by treating Christianity as the default identity of the nation and everyone else as guests, exceptions, threats, or tolerated outsiders. Non-Christians may still be legally present, but their belonging becomes conditional. They are welcome as long as they do not challenge the dominant religious story. That is not equal citizenship. That is hospitality with a house rule: remember who owns the place.

Recent survey data shows that these ideas are not fringe curiosities hiding in the political attic. PRRI’s 2025 American Values Atlas, published in 2026, found that 11 percent of Americans qualified as Christian nationalism Adherents and 21 percent as Sympathizers [@prri_mapping_christian_nationalism_2026]. The same research found that a majority of Republicans qualified as either Adherents or Sympathizers, and that white evangelical Protestants and Hispanic Protestants were the only major religious groups in which a majority qualified as Christian nationalism supporters [@prri_mapping_christian_nationalism_2026]. These numbers do not mean every Christian is a Christian nationalist, or that every evangelical supports the same political program. They do show that Christian nationalism is a measurable force in American political life, not merely a label people throw around when the discourse gets bored and wants a sharper object.

This is also why Christian nationalism should not be confused with Christianity itself. The difference matters morally and analytically. Christianity is a broad, internally diverse religious tradition with many theologies, histories, cultures, and political expressions. Christian nationalism is a political ideology that fuses Christianity with national identity and state power. It often treats one version of Christianity as the moral core of the nation and frames political opposition as rebellion against that sacred order. In that move, faith becomes less a path of discipleship and more a border wall around belonging.

The emotional power of Christian nationalism comes from loss. Many Americans who support religious nationalism believe the country has drifted away from its proper moral foundation. They may experience pluralism not as democratic equality, but as displacement. Secularism feels like hostility. Religious diversity feels like decline. LGBTQ rights, reproductive autonomy, immigration, racial justice, and changing gender roles may be interpreted as evidence that the nation has abandoned God. In that atmosphere, politics becomes restoration. The goal is not merely to pass policies; it is to take the country back. Back from whom is the question that tends to make the room go quiet.

That restoration narrative is powerful because it transforms political desire into sacred duty. If the nation is imagined as Christian property, then efforts to preserve Christian dominance can be framed as defense rather than aggression. Laws restricting abortion, LGBTQ rights, classroom discussion, school materials, or public recognition of minority identities can be described as protecting children, restoring morality, or defending religious freedom. The language is soothing. The effect is not. When one group’s restoration requires another group’s restriction, the politics of protection becomes the politics of control wearing a sweater vest.

Christian nationalism also changes how opponents are understood. In ordinary democracy, political opponents are fellow citizens with different views. In religious-national politics, opponents can become enemies of God, enemies of the family, enemies of the nation, or agents of moral corruption. This is where disagreement becomes spiritualized. A debate about curriculum becomes a battle over innocence. A debate about abortion becomes a battle between life and evil. A debate about LGBTQ rights becomes a battle over civilization itself. Once politics is framed this way, compromise becomes betrayal. You do not negotiate with demons. You defeat them. That is a terrible foundation for democratic life, unless the goal is to turn every school board meeting into a tiny apocalypse.

This dynamic helps explain why Christian nationalism often overlaps with performative Christianity. The public performance of faith supplies the symbols; Christian nationalism supplies the political story. The Bible, the flag, the church, the family, the soldier, the child, and the nation are woven into a single moral image. That image is emotionally potent because it collapses several loyalties into one. To criticize the political agenda is made to look like an attack on faith, family, patriotism, and innocence all at once. That is not an argument. It is a hostage situation with better graphic design.

The danger grows when this fusion of Christianity and nation enters law. Religious citizens may advocate for policies shaped by their beliefs, but Christian nationalism seeks more than participation. It seeks alignment between government and a particular religious identity. The state begins to reflect one faith’s symbols, one faith’s moral priorities, and one faith’s story of national purpose. Public schools become stages for religious display. Courts become arenas for theological claims dressed as constitutional reasoning. Legislatures become tools for enforcing moral boundaries that many citizens do not share. The state does not need to declare an official church to behave like it has a favorite.

This is especially harmful to Christians who do not share the nationalist vision. Progressive Christians, Black church traditions, mainline Protestants, Catholics with different political commitments, queer Christians, immigrant churches, peace churches, and others may find their faith publicly overwritten by a louder political version of Christianity. Christian nationalism often speaks as if it represents “the Christians,” when in reality it represents a contested political theology within Christianity. That flattening is itself a form of religious harm. It steals the microphone from the diversity of Christian witness and hands it to the faction most willing to confuse volume with authority.

It also harms non-Christians and the religiously unaffiliated by making them politically suspect. Jews, Muslims, Hindus, Buddhists, Sikhs, atheists, agnostics, and others may hear Christian nationalist rhetoric not as harmless heritage language, but as a warning about their place in the civic order. If the nation is Christian, then they are something else in relation to the nation: outsiders, exceptions, tolerated minorities, or people whose equality depends on the generosity of the dominant faith. That is not religious liberty. It is majority comfort with minority vulnerability.

Christian nationalism’s defenders often argue that critics are simply attacking Christians for having political views. That objection deserves to be answered directly. Christians have every right to political views. They have every right to organize, vote, protest, run for office, advocate, and argue from moral conviction. The issue is not Christian participation. The issue is Christian supremacy. There is a difference between saying “my faith shapes my politics” and saying “my faith should define the nation’s laws and identity.” The first is democracy. The second is domination.

That distinction is the backbone of this article’s broader claim. Religious participation is democratic; religious domination is not. Christianity may inspire citizens to pursue justice, mercy, community, and reform. It may challenge public cruelty and call the nation to moral accountability. But when Christianity is fused with nationalism, it becomes less able to challenge power because it has begun to identify with power. It no longer stands outside the state asking whether the state is just. It stands beside the state asking whether the state is Christian enough. That is a dangerous trade.

Christian nationalism therefore threatens both democracy and Christianity. It threatens democracy by weakening equal citizenship and treating religious identity as a measure of national belonging. It threatens Christianity by reducing faith to political identity, cultural grievance, and control over outsiders. A faith that once called believers to love enemies can become a movement that needs enemies in order to feel alive. That is not strength. That is dependence. If Christianity must dominate others to prove itself, then its political victory has already become a spiritual failure.

This is not merely a future danger or a theoretical concern. The movement of Christian identity into public authority is already visible in fights over schools, curriculum, and religious display. In 2026, a federal appeals court ruled that Texas could require public schools to display the Ten Commandments in classrooms, a decision critics argued imposed specific religious views through government authority [@ap_texas_ten_commandments_2026]. Texas also approved a required K–12 reading list that includes Bible passages and stories beginning in the 2030–31 school year, following broader efforts to place Christian texts and symbols inside public education [@reuters_texas_bible_reading_2026; @texastribune_bible_curriculum_2026]. These developments do not mean the United States has become a theocracy, and the argument should not be made carelessly. But they do show how Christian nationalist assumptions can move from rhetoric into institutions: first as heritage, then as curriculum, then as legal requirement. The line between “recognizing influence” and “normalizing authority” is already being tested.

The proper answer is not to drive Christians out of politics. It is to refuse the idea that Christianity owns the nation. Christians may participate fully in democracy, but they must do so as citizens among citizens, not as landlords of the republic. The United States can acknowledge Christianity’s historical influence without surrendering public life to Christian authority. It can protect free exercise without establishing religious hierarchy. It can allow faith to speak without allowing faith to rule by force.

The question is not whether Christianity belongs in American history or public debate. It plainly does. The question is whether Christian identity should determine who belongs in America now. A pluralistic democracy must answer no. No faith, however influential, should become the measure of citizenship. No religious tradition should become the owner of public life. And no nation that claims liberty of conscience should confuse Christian dominance with religious freedom.


# The Limits of Religious Authority

A pluralistic democracy must distinguish between religious freedom and religious authority. Religious freedom protects the right to believe, worship, teach, gather, dissent, pray, organize, and live according to conscience. Religious authority, by contrast, becomes politically dangerous when one faith claims the power to define the rights and obligations of people who do not belong to it. The difference is not small. It is the difference between protecting conscience and assigning control.

This distinction matters because religion is not merely an opinion people keep politely in a drawer. Faith shapes identity, family, ethics, grief, moral duty, community, and hope. A democracy that respects human dignity must make room for that. Religious citizens should not be expected to become secular robots when they enter public life. They may vote according to faith, speak from faith, organize through faith, and challenge injustice because of faith. The issue is not religious participation. The issue is religious rule.

The American constitutional structure reflects this tension. The First Amendment prohibits laws “respecting an establishment of religion” while also protecting the free exercise of religion [@archives_bill_of_rights]. Those two commitments are not enemies. They depend on each other. Free exercise protects people from state hostility toward religion. Non-establishment protects people from state favoritism toward religion. Together, they create the basic democratic boundary: the state should not punish faith, but neither should it become the servant of one faith.

That boundary becomes difficult because religious freedom claims often involve conduct, not belief alone. Belief can be protected absolutely in a person’s mind and conscience. Conduct is different because conduct affects other people. A person may believe that their religion requires a particular practice, but that does not automatically mean the state must permit that practice in every circumstance. In *Reynolds v. United States*, the Supreme Court rejected the argument that religious duty exempted a person from a federal anti-bigamy law, distinguishing religious belief from conduct regulated by law [@oyez_reynolds_us]. That distinction has been debated, refined, criticized, and complicated ever since, but the core problem remains: when religious practice enters public life, it can collide with the rights, safety, and equality of others.

The danger is not that religious people ask for accommodations. Accommodation can be necessary for real religious freedom. A school may need to respect religious dress. A prison may need to allow religious diets. An employer may need to make room for Sabbath observance where doing so does not impose serious harm. Without accommodation, “religious freedom” can become hollow, protecting belief in theory while making practice impossible in real life. A pluralistic society should not be allergic to accommodation. That would be lazy secularism wearing a legal blazer.

But accommodation has limits. It becomes dangerous when it shifts the cost of one person’s religion onto someone else’s rights, body, education, healthcare, employment, or equal status. Religious freedom should protect people from being forced to violate conscience where reasonable. It should not become a tool for making other people live under someone else’s conscience. The question is not only whether a believer feels burdened. The question is also who else is being burdened when the state grants relief.

This is why the language of “religious liberty” can become slippery in politics. At its best, religious liberty protects minority faiths, dissenters, unpopular communities, and people whose conscience does not align with the state. At its worst, it becomes a shield for dominant groups seeking exemptions from laws that protect others. The same phrase can defend a Muslim prisoner’s right to religious practice, an Amish family’s educational commitments, a Christian employer’s objection to contraception coverage, or a business owner’s refusal to serve LGBTQ people. The moral meaning of the claim depends on context, power, and consequence. “Religious liberty” is not a magic phrase that makes every demand just. If it were, democracy would be less a system of rights than a coupon book for exemptions.

The Supreme Court’s free exercise cases show this difficulty. In *Employment Division v. Smith*, the Court held that the Free Exercise Clause does not generally excuse individuals from complying with neutral laws of general applicability, even when those laws incidentally burden religious practice [@oyez_smith_free_exercise]. That rule attempted to preserve the government’s ability to maintain general laws without every religious objection becoming a constitutional override. Critics have argued that the rule underprotects religious minorities, and that concern is serious. But the case also reflects a real democratic fear: if every religious objection automatically defeats public law, then law itself becomes unstable. The state would be forced to ask not only what the law requires, but whose theology gets an exception.

Other cases show why exceptions can sometimes be justified. In *Wisconsin v. Yoder*, the Supreme Court held that Wisconsin could not require Amish children to attend school beyond the eighth grade when doing so burdened the Amish community’s religious way of life [@oyez_yoder]. The case is often treated as a high-water mark for religious accommodation. It shows that free exercise can protect communities whose practices differ sharply from mainstream expectations. But even there, the case raises difficult questions. Religious accommodation may protect parents and communities, but it can also affect children whose future choices are shaped by decisions made in the name of tradition. Accommodation can protect conscience, but it can also preserve authority inside the community. Both realities deserve attention.

The issue becomes even more complicated when religious objections affect third parties. In *Burwell v. Hobby Lobby*, the Supreme Court held that the Religious Freedom Restoration Act allowed certain closely held for-profit corporations to deny contraception coverage based on the religious objections of the corporation’s owners [@oyez_hobby_lobby]. Supporters viewed the decision as a protection against government overreach into religious conscience. Critics saw it as allowing an employer’s religious beliefs to affect employees’ healthcare access. This is exactly where religious liberty becomes politically charged: one person’s exemption can become another person’s burden. The law may describe the issue as accommodation, but the person losing access may experience it as control.

That third-party burden is the key limit. Religious freedom should not mean the freedom to make someone else less free. A person may refuse a medical procedure for religious reasons. That is conscience. A state may not impose that person’s theology on every patient. That is domination. A church may teach its doctrine on marriage. That is religious exercise. A government may not make that doctrine the legal boundary of family recognition for everyone. That is religious authority. A parent may teach a child religious values. That is family and faith. A public school may not become a tool for imposing one religious tradition on all students. That is state-sponsored formation. The line is not always easy, but the principle is clear: religious freedom protects voluntary belonging; religious authority governs unwilling outsiders.

This limit is especially urgent in areas where law touches bodies, families, education, medicine, identity, and speech. These are the places where political power becomes intimate. A tax policy may frustrate someone. A law about pregnancy, gender, marriage, medical care, or classroom recognition can define the conditions of a person’s life. When religious doctrine governs these areas through law, people outside the faith experience the state not as a neutral protector, but as an enforcer of someone else’s theology. That is not pluralism. That is spiritual trespassing with official stationery.

Public justification matters here. In a pluralistic society, laws that bind everyone should be justified in terms that can be debated by everyone. This does not mean religious citizens must hide their motivations or pretend their values come from nowhere. It means that when a law restricts other people’s rights, the public justification should not depend solely on doctrines those people do not accept. A church may say, “God forbids this.” A government must do more. It must explain why a rule is necessary for public justice, safety, equality, or welfare in terms that citizens of different faiths and no faith can meaningfully contest. Otherwise, law becomes theology with enforcement powers.

This is not hostility toward religion. It is respect for the limits of government. The state is a blunt instrument. It fines, arrests, regulates, restricts, funds, licenses, punishes, and compels. Faith, at its best, works through conscience, community, witness, discipline, repentance, mercy, and moral transformation. When faith reaches for state power, it may gain force, but it risks losing humility. The state does not make religion more holy. It makes religion more dangerous to those outside it. Power is very good at offering faith a megaphone and then quietly taking over the sermon.

The limit on religious authority also protects believers. If one faith gains the power to govern others today, another faith or anti-religious state power may claim that same privilege tomorrow. Religious freedom cannot survive as a winner-take-all arrangement. It must protect the conscience of those who are not in power, not merely confirm the authority of those who are. Christians who support religious domination should ask whether they would accept the same arrangement if another religion held the majority, controlled the legislature, wrote the curriculum, and defined public morality. If the answer is no, then the principle was never religious freedom. It was religious preference.

This is why pluralism requires restraint. Religious communities may believe they possess truth. They may teach it passionately. They may order their own communal lives around it. But democratic law must account for the fact that citizens disagree about ultimate truth and still belong equally to the same political community. The state cannot resolve theological disagreement by handing one side the police power. A government that does so has not created moral order. It has created legal hierarchy.

Religious authority must therefore remain limited in public life. It may persuade, but it should not compel. It may inspire law, but it should not become the sole authority behind law. It may shape personal conscience, but it should not define the rights of those outside the faith. It may criticize society, but it should not own society. These limits are not anti-religious. They are the conditions that allow religious freedom to exist without becoming religious domination.

The central boundary is simple even when its application is difficult: faith may guide the believer, but it should not govern the unbeliever. A pluralistic democracy can respect religion without surrendering to it. It can protect churches without becoming one. It can allow religious citizens to speak without allowing religious authority to rule. That boundary is not a rejection of faith. It is a defense of conscience, equality, and democracy itself.


# Consequences for Those Outside the Dominant Faith

The consequences of religious political control are felt most sharply by those who stand outside the dominant faith, outside the dominant interpretation of that faith, or outside the moral categories that dominant faith politics recognizes as legitimate. Non-Christians, atheists, agnostics, women, LGBTQ people, religious minorities, students, patients, migrants, and dissenting Christians may all find themselves governed by doctrines they do not hold. The harm is not only legal. It is emotional, civic, bodily, and existential. When one religious worldview becomes public authority, people outside that worldview do not experience it as inspiration. They experience it as a reduction in the size of their own freedom.

This matters because the United States is religiously diverse, even though Christianity remains culturally powerful. Pew Research Center’s 2023–24 Religious Landscape Study found that 62 percent of U.S. adults identify as Christian, while about 29 percent are religiously unaffiliated, including atheists, agnostics, and people who describe their religion as “nothing in particular” [@pew_religious_identity_2025]. That means Christian language may still function as a political default, but millions of people do not live inside that faith. They are not edge cases. They are neighbors, coworkers, students, patients, voters, parents, teachers, and citizens. A democracy cannot treat them as footnotes in someone else’s religious story.

The first consequence is unequal citizenship. When Christianity becomes the assumed moral identity of the nation, people outside Christianity may be treated as less fully rooted, less trustworthy, or less authentically American. They may be legally equal on paper while culturally framed as guests in a house someone else owns. That kind of exclusion does not always announce itself with open hostility. Sometimes it appears as a prayer at a public meeting, a Bible verse in a classroom, a campaign speech about “real Americans,” or a policy defended as “traditional values” without admitting whose tradition is being privileged. The message is quiet but clear: this public world was built with someone else in mind.

Religious minorities feel this pressure acutely. Jews, Muslims, Hindus, Buddhists, Sikhs, Indigenous spiritual communities, and others may all support religious freedom, but Christian dominance often asks them to accept a public culture where Christianity is treated as the natural center. Their holidays are optional. Their practices are accommodations. Their beliefs are diversity. Christianity is tradition. That asymmetry matters. It teaches people that some faiths belong by default while others belong by permission. Nothing says “equal liberty” like having to explain that your religion is not a scheduling inconvenience.

The religiously unaffiliated face a related problem. Atheists, agnostics, and people with no religious affiliation are often treated as if they lack moral seriousness because they do not ground ethics in God. This is one of the most persistent myths in religious politics: that morality requires religious belief, and that public life becomes dangerous when secular people gain influence. But a pluralistic democracy cannot measure citizenship by theology. A person does not need to believe in God to know the difference between cruelty and care, honesty and deception, harm and help. People can be good, generous, patient, ethical, helpful, and accommodating without needing religion as their moral supervisor. A person does not need to believe in God to care about justice, dignity, compassion, or the common good. Moral seriousness is not issued at baptism.

Women and people who can become pregnant experience religious political control most directly through laws governing reproduction. When one religious interpretation of fetal life, sexuality, and gender becomes law, the state gains power over pregnancy, miscarriage, abortion, medical emergencies, and family planning. After *Dobbs*, abortion access became radically uneven across the country; Guttmacher reports that 41 states have abortion bans in effect with limited exceptions, including 13 states with total abortion bans and 28 with gestational-duration bans [@guttmacher_abortion_bans_2026]. For people living under those laws, theological disagreement is not theoretical. It can determine whether they receive medical care, whether they must remain pregnant, whether they travel across state lines, and whether their own moral judgment matters less than a legislature’s religiously shaped assumptions.

The bodily consequence is profound. A person may not share the religious belief that life begins at conception. A doctor may understand pregnancy through medical risk rather than theology. A family may face a wanted pregnancy that becomes dangerous or nonviable. But when religious doctrine shapes law, the state can force everyone into one moral framework. The result is not simply “a policy disagreement.” It is compulsory theology experienced through the body. The law does not merely express values. It enters the clinic, the emergency room, the marriage, the bedroom, and the grief of people already facing some of the hardest decisions of their lives.

LGBTQ people experience a similar conversion of religious morality into civic vulnerability. When conservative Christian politics frames queer and trans identities as threats to children, family, or civilization, LGBTQ people become symbols in someone else’s moral drama. Their pronouns, books, healthcare, marriages, bathrooms, classrooms, sports teams, and family structures become political evidence. The Movement Advancement Project LGBTQ curricular-law tracker documents state policies involving LGBTQ inclusion and exclusion in school curricula, including “Don’t Say LGBTQ” laws, older curriculum-censorship laws, and parental opt-in or opt-out policies [@map_lgbtq_curricular_laws]. The issue is not merely whether one lesson appears in a classroom. It is whether LGBTQ students and families are allowed to exist publicly without being treated as contamination.

This kind of policy produces emotional consequences that law alone does not capture. When a student hears that their family cannot be mentioned, that their identity is inappropriate, or that adults must protect other children from knowing people like them exist, the state teaches shame. When a trans person becomes the subject of legislative debate year after year, the state teaches precarity. When queer families are described as threats to religious liberty, the state teaches that their equality is someone else’s burden. A law can be written in neutral language and still land like a verdict on someone’s humanity. Bureaucracy has excellent handwriting, but it can still be cruel.

Conversion therapy belongs in this same pattern because it turns religious disapproval into a project of correction. Whether aimed at youth or adults, the premise is that queer or trans identity is a defect to be repaired rather than a life to be understood with dignity. The American Psychological Association describes sexual orientation and gender identity change efforts as ineffective and potentially harmful [@apa_conversion_therapy_evidence_2025]. The Movement Advancement Project tracks conversion therapy laws across the United States, including laws that restrict licensed mental-health providers from subjecting minors to conversion therapy practices [@map_conversion_therapy_laws_2026]. For minors, the danger is especially acute because family, church, school, and professional authority can combine into coercion. A young person may be told that love, housing, belonging, or salvation depends on becoming someone else. For adults, the pressure may be less legally direct but still spiritually and emotionally powerful: a person may be pushed toward “healing,” “restoration,” or “deliverance” by a community they cannot afford to lose. Trevor Project research has connected recent exposure to conversion therapy with higher reported suicidal thoughts and attempts among LGBTQ young people [@trevor_conversion_therapy_suicidality_2026]. The harm is not only that conversion efforts fail to change who people are. The harm is that they teach people to experience themselves as broken, sinful, shameful, or unacceptable until they become more comfortable for others. That is not care. It is rejection with a counseling vocabulary.

The stakes can become deadly. This should be said carefully, because suicide is never caused by one factor alone. But rejection, shame, homelessness, bullying, hostile laws, conversion efforts, and family abandonment can all become part of the pressure placed on already vulnerable young people. LGBTQ youth who are rejected by their families or pushed out of their homes do not experience that rejection as an abstract theological position. They experience it as the loss of safety, shelter, love, and belonging. The Trevor Project’s 2025 national survey found that 36 percent of LGBTQ young people seriously considered attempting suicide in the past year, and 10 percent attempted suicide [@trevor_lgbtq_youth_mental_health_2025]. Its research on homelessness and housing instability found that LGBTQ youth who had experienced housing instability reported attempting suicide at 3.5 times the rate of LGBTQ youth who had not [@trevor_lgbtq_youth_homelessness_2022]. When parents throw out their children in the name of faith, they may call it conviction, discipline, or obedience. But a Christianity that abandons a child at the moment that child most needs love has not defended the Bible. It has betrayed the very mercy, welcome, and care for the vulnerable that the Bible repeatedly demands [@bible_matthew_25_35_40].

Pew Research Center’s 2025 survey of LGBTQ adults underscores that these are not abstract categories but lived experiences, documenting how LGBTQ Americans describe their lives, identities, challenges, and hopes in the current United States [@pew_lgbtq_experiences_2025]. The policy debate often talks about LGBTQ people as issues. But people do not live as issues. They live as children trying to survive school, adults trying to keep jobs, parents trying to protect families, patients trying to receive care, and citizens trying to move through public life without becoming a campaign prop. Nothing says “family values” like turning someone else’s family into a warning label.

Students are especially vulnerable because public schools carry state authority. When religiously framed politics shapes curriculum, school libraries, history standards, sex education, or classroom discussion, students are not merely receiving information. They are being told which lives count as normal, which histories matter, and which questions are safe to ask. Religious domination in schools can harm religious minorities by making them feel like outsiders, LGBTQ students by making them invisible or suspect, and secular students by implying that morality belongs to religion alone. A public school should not require a child to translate the state’s values through someone else’s theology before they can feel like they belong.

Children absorb these hierarchies quickly. A child does not need a constitutional theory to understand when their family is treated as strange, when their holiday is ignored, when their identity is whispered around, or when their religion is presented as something other than the national default. They learn belonging through repetition: whose prayers are normal, whose stories are central, whose parents are respected, whose questions are welcomed, and whose existence requires explanation. When religious dominance enters public institutions, it does not only regulate adults. It teaches children where they stand.

Patients and families also bear consequences when religious authority shapes medical access. This includes abortion and reproductive care, but it extends beyond them. Religious doctrine can influence debates over contraception, fertility treatment, end-of-life care, gender-affirming care, stem-cell research, and hospital policy. The danger is not that religious institutions have ethical commitments. They do. The danger is when those commitments govern patients who do not share them, especially when religiously affiliated healthcare systems serve the general public. A patient in crisis should not have to discover at the worst moment of their life that their medical options have been narrowed by theology they never consented to.

Dissenting Christians also suffer under dominant religious politics. Progressive Christians, queer Christians, Black church traditions, immigrant churches, peace churches, mainline Protestants, Catholics with different political commitments, and others may find their faith publicly misrepresented by a louder and more politically organized version of Christianity. When one faction claims to speak for “Christian values,” it often erases the diversity of Christian witness. Christians who emphasize poverty, racial justice, peacemaking, immigrant care, environmental stewardship, or LGBTQ inclusion may be treated as fake Christians, compromised Christians, or Christians who have surrendered to secular culture. The dominant faith does not only exclude outsiders. It disciplines insiders who refuse the script.

This is one reason religious domination is so corrosive: it turns pluralism inside the faith into betrayal. Christianity itself becomes smaller when one political interpretation claims ownership of the whole tradition. The Black church’s liberation witness, Catholic social teaching, Anabaptist peace traditions, mainline Protestant reform movements, queer Christian theology, and other forms of Christian ethical life cannot be flattened into one culture-war platform without violence to the tradition’s complexity. When political Christianity claims the microphone, it often calls that unity. It is not unity. It is a volume problem.

The harm also reaches migrants and racial minorities when Christian identity is fused with national belonging. Christian nationalist politics often imagines the nation through a nostalgic moral order that is not only religious, but racialized and cultural. The “real America” invoked in these movements is often implicitly white, English-speaking, heterosexual, patriarchal, and Christian. Even when the language is formally about religion, the emotional picture is larger than doctrine. It is about who looks like they belong, whose families are treated as normal, whose grief is legible, whose accent sounds threatening, and whose presence is framed as change rather than contribution.

The emotional burden of this kind of politics is difficult to overstate. People outside the dominant faith may learn to edit themselves in public. They may hide religious symbols, avoid mentioning partners, soften political speech, avoid school conflict, travel for healthcare, change names, explain holidays, or calculate whether honesty will cost them safety. That calculation is exhausting. Equal citizenship should not require constant self-protection. Yet religious domination teaches people to ask, again and again: how much of myself can I reveal before someone else’s theology becomes my problem?

This is one of the quietest consequences of religious domination: it teaches people to manage the majority’s comfort. Those outside the dominant faith learn which parts of themselves are safe to show, which questions will make the room tense, which beliefs need softening, and which truths are better saved for private spaces. They become fluent in self-editing. The atheist learns not to sound “too atheist.” The queer student learns not to sound “too visible.” The Muslim coworker learns not to sound “too demanding.” The Jewish family learns to explain why the public calendar does not fit their life. The dissenting Christian learns that disagreement will be treated as betrayal. This is not equal belonging. It is conditional belonging, and conditional belonging is just exclusion with better manners.

This is why the phrase “religious freedom” must be examined by its effects. If religious freedom means that believers may practice, worship, speak, organize, and live according to conscience, it is essential. If religious freedom means that one group may use law to restrict the bodies, speech, education, healthcare, and families of others, it has become something else. Freedom for the dominant faith can become captivity for everyone outside it. The name remains beautiful. The function changes.

A society shaped by religious domination may still call itself free because it allows dissent in theory. But freedom in theory is thin comfort when law, culture, and public institutions repeatedly tell people that their lives are morally suspect. A person may technically be free to disagree with the dominant faith while still being forced to live under its policies. That is the difference between formal liberty and lived liberty. Formal liberty says, “You may believe what you want.” Lived liberty asks, “Can you make medical decisions, raise your family, attend school, marry, speak, work, and belong without someone else’s doctrine standing over you?”

The people outside the dominant faith do not experience religious political control as a philosophical puzzle. They experience it in paperwork, waiting rooms, classrooms, courtrooms, family arguments, school board meetings, job policies, insurance coverage, and campaign ads. They experience it when their bodies become legal territory, when their families become moral threats, when their identities become curriculum disputes, when their religions become exceptions, and when their citizenship becomes conditional. The injury is not only that rights are restricted. It is that the restriction is framed as righteousness.

That is why this issue cannot be softened into a polite disagreement about values. Values are not harmless when they are backed by law. A dominant religious group may say it is defending morality, but those outside the group may experience that defense as surveillance, exclusion, and control. The moral question must therefore be asked from the position of the governed, not only from the position of the believer. A law inspired by faith cannot be judged only by the sincerity of those who support it. It must also be judged by the freedom of those who endure it.

When faith becomes law, those outside the dominant faith become the testing ground for its power. Their bodies reveal whether religious liberty is truly liberty or merely privilege. Their classrooms reveal whether public education belongs to everyone or only to the majority’s moral imagination. Their families reveal whether equality is real or conditional. Their fear reveals what the law is doing beneath its language. And their resistance reveals the truth that domination always tries to hide: no faith has the right to make unwilling citizens live as though they belong to it.


# Global and Interreligious Comparison

The danger of religious authority becoming political power is not unique to Christianity. Christianity is the central concern of this article because of its historical and contemporary influence in the United States, but the broader pattern is global and interreligious. Across traditions and political systems, freedom shrinks when religious identity, state authority, nationalism, and moral certainty become inseparable. Sometimes religion captures the state. Sometimes the state captures religion. Sometimes a majority faith becomes the nation’s preferred identity. The direction of control may change, but the result is familiar: belief becomes boundary, dissent becomes danger, and people outside the approved order become easier to govern, punish, or erase.

This comparison matters because criticism of politicized Christianity is often misread as hostility toward Christianity itself. But the issue is not Christianity as a faith. The issue is domination as a political form. Islam, Hinduism, Buddhism, Judaism, Christianity, and other traditions have all produced communities of compassion, learning, reform, service, beauty, resistance, and moral courage. They have also, in particular historical and political settings, been used to justify hierarchy, exclusion, censorship, violence, nationalism, and control. That does not make all religions identical, nor does it erase differences in theology, history, or political context. It shows something less comforting: no religious tradition is automatically protected from political corruption, and no government becomes harmless merely because it claims sacred purpose.

Global research on religious restrictions reinforces the seriousness of this concern. Pew Research Center has tracked government restrictions on religion and social hostilities involving religion across nearly two hundred countries and territories [@pew_global_religious_restrictions_2024; @pew_religious_restrictions_feature_2026]. The importance of this research is not merely statistical. It demonstrates that religious restriction is not an American culture-war abstraction. Around the world, governments regulate worship, privilege official traditions, punish dissent, suppress minority communities, police religious identity, and decide which forms of faith may appear in public. The vocabulary varies. The machinery does not.

Iran offers one of the clearest examples of religious authority controlling the state. The government’s official interpretation of Shi’a Islam shapes law, political legitimacy, gender regulation, public morality, and the treatment of religious minorities and dissenters. The United States Commission on International Religious Freedom has documented systematic repression affecting Baha’is, Christians, Jews, Sunni Muslims, Sufis, converts, dissidents, and others who fall outside the state’s approved religious order [@uscirf_iran_2026]. In such a system, political disagreement can become religious disobedience, and religious dissent can become a threat to the government itself.

The point is not that Islam inherently produces theocracy. Muslims live within an enormous range of political, cultural, and theological traditions. The danger arises when a government claims divine legitimacy for one interpretation and equips that interpretation with police, courts, prisons, and censorship. Once that happens, the state does not merely protect religion. It determines which religion is legitimate. Believers remain free only to the extent that their belief agrees with the government’s theology. The state becomes both ruler and unofficial editor of God.

Afghanistan under the Taliban reveals the same danger in an even more intimate form. Taliban authorities enforce a severe interpretation of Sunni Islam that has been used to restrict dissent, religious minorities, women, and girls [@uscirf_afghanistan_2026]. Restrictions on education, employment, movement, dress, speech, and public participation demonstrate how quickly theological authority can enter everyday life. It does not remain in courts, sermons, or official decrees. It follows people into classrooms, workplaces, streets, homes, clothing, and bodies.

These examples make the danger of theocracy visible, but the opposite arrangement can be equally coercive. China demonstrates what happens when the state does not submit to religion but instead demands that religion submit to the state. The Chinese Communist Party is officially atheist, yet it does not simply leave religion alone. Religious groups must conform to party ideology and operate through an extensive structure of state recognition, registration, surveillance, regulation, and political supervision [@uscirf_china_state_control_2026].

China officially recognizes Buddhism, Catholicism, Islam, Protestantism, and Taoism, but legal religious activity is managed through state-controlled organizations. Religious communities that refuse this control may face harassment, detention, closure, surveillance, or other punishment. Uyghur Muslims, Tibetan Buddhists, house-church Christians, underground Catholics, Falun Gong practitioners, and other communities have faced severe repression [@uscirf_china_religious_freedom_2026]. Under the policy commonly described as the “Sinicization of religion,” religious institutions and teachings are expected to align with Chinese Communist Party leadership, political ideology, and state-defined cultural identity.

China is an important comparison because it prevents the argument from becoming too narrow. The danger is not only religion taking control of government. Government can also take control of religion. A Christian theocracy may demand that law conform to Christianity. An authoritarian communist state may demand that Christianity, Islam, Buddhism, and every other faith conform to the party. One places the sacred above the state. The other places the state above the sacred. Both arrangements deny individuals and communities the freedom to interpret, question, worship, dissent, and organize outside centralized authority.

This mirror image reveals the deeper principle: concentrated power does not care which direction the leash runs. Whether religious leaders command the state or state officials command religion, conscience becomes subordinate to an institution that claims the right to define acceptable belief. Faith is no longer free to challenge political power because political power has either absorbed it or placed it under supervision. The government may allow worship, but only after approving the guest list, editing the sermon, checking the camera feed, and making sure heaven has completed the proper registration form.

Hindu nationalism offers a different but related pattern. India is constitutionally secular, internally diverse, and home to vast and varied Hindu traditions. Yet Hindu nationalist politics has increasingly framed Indian identity through majoritarian religious and cultural terms. Human-rights organizations have documented discriminatory policies, inflammatory rhetoric, unequal enforcement, and violence affecting Muslims, Christians, and other minorities under the Bharatiya Janata Party’s political dominance [@hrw_india_religious_minorities_2026; @freedomhouse_india_2026].

Again, the issue is not Hinduism as a religion. The issue is the conversion of majority religion into a theory of national ownership. When Hindu identity is treated as the natural center of Indian nationhood, citizens outside that identity may remain legally present while becoming culturally conditional. Their citizenship may be recognized in law but questioned in rhetoric, policing, employment, housing, elections, or everyday public life. The majority religion becomes heritage. Minority religion becomes suspicion.

That conditionality is one of the most consistent features of religious nationalism. A government does not need to revoke citizenship outright to weaken belonging. It can require minorities to demonstrate loyalty, tolerate surveillance, accept unequal treatment, endure public suspicion, and watch the majority’s religion become the emotional language of the state. The minority remains a citizen, but citizenship begins to feel like probation.

Myanmar presents an especially severe example of religion, ethnicity, and nationalism becoming fused into exclusion. Buddhist nationalism and anti-Muslim rhetoric helped shape a political environment in which the Rohingya, a predominantly Muslim ethnic minority, were denied citizenship, portrayed as foreign intruders, subjected to segregation, and targeted by state violence. Myanmar’s military operations against Rohingya communities included killings, sexual violence, destruction of villages, forced displacement, and other atrocities [@ohchr_myanmar_fact_finding_genocide; @us_state_rohingya_genocide_2022].

The United States formally determined in 2022 that members of Myanmar’s military committed genocide and crimes against humanity against the Rohingya [@us_state_rohingya_genocide_2022]. A United Nations fact-finding mission concluded that senior military leaders should be investigated and prosecuted for genocide, crimes against humanity, and war crimes [@ohchr_myanmar_fact_finding_genocide]. The Gambia’s case accusing Myanmar of violating the Genocide Convention remains before the International Court of Justice, which has ordered provisional measures to protect the Rohingya while the litigation proceeds [@icj_gambia_myanmar_genocide_case]. The final legal judgment on the merits remains pending, but describing the atrocities as genocide is not merely rhetorical; it reflects formal governmental determinations, international investigations, and an active case under the Genocide Convention.

Accuracy also requires acknowledging that Rohingya armed actors have committed violence. The Arakan Rohingya Salvation Army carried out coordinated attacks against security posts in 2016 and 2017, and Amnesty International documented killings and abductions attributed to ARSA fighters, including attacks against Hindu civilians [@amnesty_arsa_abuses_2018]. Those crimes should not be denied or romanticized. A minority community does not become morally infallible because it is persecuted.

But crimes committed by an armed group do not justify collective punishment of an entire ethnic and religious population. ARSA is not every Rohingya man, woman, or child. An attack on security forces cannot justify mass rape, village destruction, citizenship denial, forced displacement, or genocidal violence against civilians. A government that responds to militants by treating an entire people as the enemy has abandoned security and embraced collective guilt. That distinction matters everywhere: accountability must follow individual responsibility, not religious or ethnic identity.

The Rohingya case therefore illustrates two truths at once. Members of a persecuted population can commit serious crimes, and that population can still be subjected to genocide. Acknowledging the first does not erase the second. Moral analysis fails when it demands perfectly innocent victims before condemning mass atrocity. Human rights are not prizes awarded only to communities whose every member behaves well. If they were, no nation on Earth would qualify.

Buddhism is often presented in Western popular culture as inherently peaceful, meditative, and detached from political violence. Myanmar exposes the limits of that simplified image. Buddhist teachings may emphasize compassion and non-harm, yet Buddhist identity can still be recruited into ethnic nationalism, fear, territorial belonging, and political exclusion. No tradition is protected by its public reputation. Sacred language can speak softly while standing beside a locked gate, a military checkpoint, or a burning village.

Israel and Palestine present another complex case in which religion, ethnicity, history, territory, trauma, security, and nationalism overlap. Any serious discussion must begin by acknowledging Jewish histories of persecution, exile, antisemitism, pogroms, and genocide. Jewish vulnerability is not invented, and Jewish self-determination cannot be evaluated honestly without recognizing the historical conditions that shaped it. Antisemitism remains real and dangerous, and criticism of Israeli policy must never become a disguise for hostility toward Jewish people.

At the same time, historical vulnerability does not make state power immune from moral scrutiny. In the West Bank, Israeli settlers hold Israeli citizenship and civil rights that are not equally available to Palestinians living under military occupation. Palestinians face restrictions involving movement, land, construction, political participation, home demolition, detention, and access to resources, while settlement expansion deepens unequal territorial and legal realities [@freedomhouse_west_bank_2026]. Religious and historical claims to land can become entangled with state authority in ways that privilege one population while constraining another.

The issue is not Judaism as a faith, nor is it the existence of Jewish communal or national life. The issue is whether religious-national claims can justify permanent inequality, displacement, or domination. Jewish safety and Palestinian freedom should not be treated as mutually exclusive moral goods. A political order that protects one people by indefinitely denying equal dignity and self-determination to another has not solved historical vulnerability. It has redistributed it.

This same caution should apply across every example. Criticizing religious nationalism is not the same as attacking religious people. Antisemitism, Islamophobia, anti-Hindu hatred, anti-Christian persecution, anti-Buddhist violence, and hostility toward other religious communities are all real and dangerous. A community can be persecuted in one setting and politically dominant in another. It may deserve protection from hatred while also requiring accountability when its institutions, symbols, or political movements are used to harm others. History refuses to keep anyone in one tidy moral category. Very inconsiderate of it.

Interreligious comparison must therefore be used carefully. The point is not to rank religions by danger or construct a scoreboard of suffering. The examples are not interchangeable. Iran is not China. China is not India. India is not Myanmar. Myanmar is not Israel and Palestine. Their histories, legal systems, religious traditions, and political conflicts differ dramatically.

The point is to identify recurring political mechanisms. A religious or ideological identity becomes a claim to national ownership. National ownership becomes a claim to legal privilege. Legal privilege becomes a mechanism for controlling outsiders and dissenters. Alternatively, a government declares itself the sole guardian of public order, forces religious communities to submit to state ideology, and punishes spiritual independence as political disobedience. In one system, religion captures government. In another, government captures religion. Either way, conscience loses.

The vocabulary changes from place to place. One movement invokes Christian civilization. Another invokes Islamic virtue. Another invokes Hindu nationhood. Another invokes Buddhist protection of the homeland. Another invokes sacred land and national security. An authoritarian state may instead invoke social harmony, national unity, modernization, or ideological loyalty. These claims emerge from different histories and cannot simply be equated. But each can become dangerous when it turns communal meaning into coercive authority.

Religious and ideological domination also tend to describe themselves as defensive. They rarely announce, “We would like to oppress people now.” They say they are protecting tradition, children, security, purity, civilization, harmony, sovereignty, national identity, or divine order. Sometimes the fear underneath those claims is sincere. Communities may fear secularization, demographic change, violence, cultural erasure, humiliation, foreign influence, or loss of political control. But fear does not automatically make coercion just. A majority’s anxiety cannot become the legal ceiling on minority freedom.

This matters for the American argument because Christian nationalism often presents itself as uniquely innocent. It claims merely to restore moral order, protect religious freedom, or honor national heritage. But global comparison shows that dominant movements nearly always describe power as protection. Majority faiths call privilege tradition. Theocracies call enforcement virtue. Nationalists call exclusion security. Authoritarian states call surveillance harmony. The language may sound reassuring, but the better question is always the same: who becomes less free when this movement gains authority?

The comparison also strengthens the article’s principle by making it universal. If Christian domination is wrong only because Christianity is involved, the argument fails. The stronger claim is that no religious or ideological authority should possess unchecked power over conscience. Christian law imposed on non-Christians is wrong. Islamic theocracy imposed on dissenters and minorities is wrong. Hindu nationalism that makes Muslims and Christians conditional citizens is wrong. Buddhist nationalism that marks Muslims as alien is wrong. Jewish religious nationalism used to justify unequal rights or territorial domination is wrong. An officially atheist state that imprisons, edits, surveils, or restructures religious life is also wrong.

The principle must remain consistent or it is not a principle. It is preference wearing an academic name tag.

That consistency matters because religious freedom must protect everyone or it will eventually protect no one. Christians who support government-sponsored Christianity should consider whether they would accept state-sponsored Islam. Muslims who favor Islamic legal supremacy should consider whether they would accept Hindu nationalist rule. Secular citizens who oppose theocracy should also oppose an authoritarian government deciding which churches may exist, which imams may preach, which Buddhist leaders are legitimate, and which sacred texts may be taught.

The objection to domination cannot depend on whether the dominant worldview is familiar. The universal rule must be that no one should be forced to live under a religious or ideological authority they do not share, except where democratically enacted law is justified through public reasons and constrained by equal rights. Conscience cannot belong to the majority. It cannot belong to clergy. It cannot belong to a party. It cannot belong to the state.

The global lesson is therefore broader than religion alone. Faith can enrich public life when it speaks through conscience, service, moral imagination, solidarity, truth-telling, and resistance to cruelty. Secular government can protect freedom when it remains accountable, pluralistic, and limited. But religion fused with state power can become coercive, and a state determined to dominate religion can become equally destructive. The deeper danger is concentrated power wrapped in unquestionable moral certainty.

When political authority declares itself sacred, historically destined, culturally pure, ideologically infallible, or uniquely entitled to define belonging, freedom begins to contract. Belief becomes boundary. Identity becomes hierarchy. Difference becomes disloyalty. Outsiders become problems. Resistance becomes sin, treason, extremism, impurity, or corruption.

That is why democracies must protect religious life while limiting religious authority, and protect public order without turning the state into the owner of conscience. The goal is not a society without faith. It is a society in which faith remains free because no church, mosque, temple, monastery, party, military, ethnic majority, or government ministry has the power to make everyone else kneel.

The examples in this section differ in history, theology, culture, and political structure. They should not be flattened into moral equivalents, nor should they be treated as identical in scale or severity. Their importance lies elsewhere. They demonstrate that the danger is remarkably consistent regardless of which religion—or even which ideology—holds power. Whenever a government claims authority because it speaks for God, or whenever it claims authority over God, liberty contracts. The names change. The symbols change. The scriptures change. The flags change. The mechanism does not. Every society eventually arrives at the same question: **Who decides what every other citizen must believe, and what happens when they refuse?**


# Counterargument: Religious Participation Versus Religious Domination

A serious argument about religion and political power must confront the strongest objection directly: religious people are citizens, and citizens do not surrender their deepest convictions when they enter public life. Faith shapes moral judgment, political priorities, community obligations, and ideas about justice. Christians, Muslims, Jews, Hindus, Buddhists, atheists, agnostics, and others all bring comprehensive moral beliefs into democratic debate. It would be neither possible nor desirable to demand that religious citizens speak only in secular language or pretend that their convictions have no influence on their politics.

Religious participation has often strengthened democratic life. Faith communities have organized against slavery, supported civil-rights movements, sheltered migrants, opposed war, fed the hungry, defended prisoners, built hospitals, educated children, and challenged governments that treated cruelty as policy. Religious leaders and communities have sometimes supplied the moral language necessary to expose injustice when legal language alone proved too bloodless. A democracy that silenced religious conviction would lose an important source of conscience, courage, solidarity, and reform.

This is why the argument of this article cannot be that religion should disappear from public life. Such a demand would be anti-democratic and practically incoherent. Secular citizens are not required to hide the philosophies that shape their political beliefs. Religious citizens should not be required to hide theirs. A Christian may oppose poverty because of the teachings of Jesus. A Jewish citizen may advocate for refugees because of religious memory and law. A Muslim may support charitable policy because of obligations to justice and care. A Buddhist may oppose violence because of commitments to non-harm. These motivations are not democratic defects. They are examples of citizens bringing moral seriousness into public debate.

The First Amendment reflects this double protection. It prohibits religious establishment while safeguarding free exercise [@archives_bill_of_rights]. Those principles do not require religious silence. They require governmental restraint. The state may not suppress faith simply because faith influences political action, but neither may it elevate one faith into official authority. Religious citizens may enter the public square fully clothed in conviction. The government simply may not hand one of them the keys and announce that everyone else now lives in their sanctuary.

Supporters of greater religious influence in politics often argue that secularism is not neutral. They are correct in one important respect. Every political system rests on moral assumptions. Laws against violence, theft, discrimination, abuse, and exploitation express judgments about human dignity, responsibility, freedom, and harm. Public education reflects beliefs about knowledge and citizenship. Social policy reflects judgments about obligation and fairness. There is no political order completely free of moral values.

That point deserves to be taken seriously because calls for “neutrality” can sometimes conceal dominant secular assumptions. Religious citizens may reasonably fear that the exclusion of religious language from public debate will not create a neutral society but a society in which only secular moral reasoning receives official respect. A government hostile to religion can be as coercive as one captured by religion. China’s control over religious life demonstrates that secular or officially atheist authority is not automatically pluralistic. The state can oppress faith just as faith can capture the state.

But the fact that perfect neutrality is impossible does not mean all forms of influence are equally legitimate. A democracy may be morally structured without being religiously owned. The relevant question is not whether law reflects values. Of course it does. The question is whether those values can be publicly debated, challenged, revised, and applied without requiring citizens to accept one religious authority as true.

A religious person may argue that poverty policy should reflect the dignity of every human being because all people are created by God. A secular person may support the same policy because suffering should be reduced and equality protected. They do not need to share the same ultimate reason in order to build a common law. Democratic politics frequently works through overlapping conclusions reached from different moral starting points. The problem begins when the religious citizen says that the law must prevail because God commands it and that dissenters therefore have no legitimate standing to object.

The civil-rights movement provides an important test of this distinction. Martin Luther King Jr. did not hide the religious foundations of his moral vision. Black churches did not translate themselves into theological silence before confronting segregation. They invoked scripture, divine justice, human dignity, constitutional promises, and the lived consequences of racial oppression. But their public argument did not require every American to accept Christian theology before recognizing the injustice of segregation. Their religious witness was joined to evidence, constitutional principle, equal citizenship, and an appeal to freedoms that could be claimed by believers and nonbelievers alike. Most importantly, the movement sought to widen the circle of citizenship rather than place outsiders under sectarian control. Religious language used to demand equal freedom is not politically equivalent to religious language used to restrict it.

Religious arguments do not become illegitimate merely because they are religious. They become politically insufficient when they are used as the sole justification for coercing people who do not recognize the claimed religious authority. “My faith teaches me to do this” can be a complete reason for personal conduct. It is not always a complete reason for making everyone else do it. Once the state becomes involved, the argument must address public consequences, rights, evidence, equality, and harm.

This distinction can be seen in debates over abortion, sexuality, education, and family life. A religious community may teach that abortion is morally wrong. Its members may refuse abortion, advocate against it, support pregnant people, establish charities, and attempt to persuade others. That is religious participation. When the community uses state power to compel people outside the faith to follow its doctrine, despite disagreement about personhood, medicine, and bodily autonomy, participation moves toward domination.

A church may teach that marriage is between a man and a woman. It may govern its own sacraments accordingly. That is religious exercise. When it demands that civil marriage for everyone conform to its doctrine, it seeks authority over families outside the church. A parent may teach a child that gender and sexuality should be understood through religious belief. That is family freedom. When public schools are required to erase other families, identities, or bodies of knowledge to preserve that parent’s religious comfort, the cost is transferred to every other child.

The distinction is not always emotionally satisfying because democratic pluralism rarely gives anyone complete moral victory. Religious citizens may sincerely believe that permitting conduct they regard as sinful implicates society in wrongdoing. Secular citizens may sincerely believe that religious exemptions or restrictions undermine equality. Both may feel that compromise asks them to tolerate injustice. Democracy cannot eliminate that tension. It can only prevent one side from resolving it by ruling the other.

This restraint can be painful, and the pain should not be dismissed as imaginary simply because a democratic limit is justified. A believer may sincerely feel that society is permitting conduct they regard as profoundly wrong. A secular citizen may feel that religious accommodation gives public legitimacy to ideas that threaten their equality or autonomy. Members of historically targeted minorities may reasonably distrust calls for patience, civility, or compromise when those words have too often meant accepting one more restriction, one more delay, or one more demand to make the majority comfortable.

Democracy does not require pretending that these experiences are emotionally equivalent. Nor does it require treating every disagreement as harmless. Some compromises preserve coexistence; others merely ask vulnerable people to absorb the consequences of someone else’s certainty. A useful distinction is between losing the power to control another person and losing control over one’s own life. A religious group may experience legal recognition of conduct it opposes as a moral defeat. But the person whose marriage, healthcare, education, identity, or family is restricted experiences something materially different: the state has entered their life and reduced their choices.

That difference must remain visible. Being unable to make one’s neighbors obey a religious teaching is not the same as being forbidden to follow that teaching oneself. Feeling offended, displaced, or morally distressed by another person’s freedom is not equivalent to losing bodily autonomy, equal protection, family recognition, medical access, or public belonging. The discomfort of pluralism can be genuine without becoming a constitutional injury.

This does not mean the majority’s convictions are unimportant or that religious people must celebrate every legal freedom they oppose. It means they must distinguish between tolerating another person’s choices and being compelled to make those choices themselves. Democratic restraint asks citizens to live beside conduct they may condemn, just as everyone else must live beside beliefs they may find harmful, irrational, or unjust. That mutual frustration is part of sharing a country with people who do not answer to the same authority.

The line is crossed when one group treats its moral distress as sufficient reason to narrow another group’s freedom. At that point, compromise stops being mutual and becomes directional: one side retains its beliefs and practices, while the other side loses rights, safety, recognition, or control over its own life. The language may still be “balance,” but the burden is no longer balanced.

Moral certainty, however sincere, cannot grant one group unilateral authority over another. Democracy sometimes requires people to endure the pain of not winning every moral argument. It should not require vulnerable citizens to endure the harm of having their lives governed by arguments they were never free to reject. The pain of not controlling one’s neighbors is not the same as the harm of being controlled by them.

This is where the language of complicity becomes important. Religious political movements often claim that allowing others to act differently forces believers to participate in wrongdoing. Sometimes that concern is legitimate. A person should not casually be forced to perform an act that directly violates conscience when reasonable accommodation is possible. But complicity cannot be defined so broadly that another person’s independent life becomes a burden on the believer.

A Christian is not forced to enter a same-sex marriage because same-sex civil marriage is legal. A person opposed to contraception is not forced to use it because another person has access to it. A religious parent is not forced to affirm another family’s beliefs because that family appears in a school library. Living alongside people who make different moral choices is not the same as being compelled to make those choices oneself. Pluralism requires coexistence, not unanimous approval.

If moral disagreement itself is treated as coercion, then the dominant group gains a veto over everyone else’s freedom. The believer’s discomfort becomes legally actionable, while the outsider’s restricted life becomes politically invisible. That is not balance. It is a hierarchy of conscience in which one person’s objection carries more weight than another person’s autonomy.

Another common defense of religious political power is majoritarian democracy. If most citizens are Christian, the argument goes, then Christian values will naturally shape law. Democracy does allow majorities to enact policy, but constitutional democracy is not simple majority rule. Majorities are limited by rights, equal protection, due process, free exercise, and non-establishment. Otherwise, religious liberty would belong only to whichever faith won the last census.

The purpose of constitutional rights is most visible when a majority dislikes how a minority lives. Popular speech does not need much protection. Familiar religion does not need much accommodation. Majority culture rarely has to ask whether it belongs. Rights matter because they restrain power at the moment power feels most justified in using itself.

A Christian majority may believe its values are beneficial, compassionate, and true. That sincerity does not erase the danger of legal privilege. Every dominant religious movement tends to understand its authority as benevolent. It does not imagine itself imposing. It imagines itself guiding, restoring, protecting, or healing. But the people being guided may experience surveillance. The people being restored may experience erasure. The people being protected may wonder why protection feels so much like losing rights.

Good intentions are therefore not enough. Religious laws must be judged by what they do, not only by what supporters believe they mean. A policy may be offered as protection for children while teaching some children that their families are shameful. It may be offered as protection for life while denying patients medically necessary care. It may be offered as protection for religious liberty while transferring religious obligations onto workers, students, patients, or customers who do not share them.

This does not mean every law influenced by religion is illegitimate. Religious conviction may inspire laws against exploitation, violence, poverty, discrimination, or environmental destruction. The relevant test is not whether faith played a role in motivation. The test is whether the law can be defended through public reasons, respects equal citizenship, and avoids imposing sectarian doctrine as such.

A useful democratic standard is therefore threefold. First, religious citizens must remain free to advocate, organize, persuade, vote, and serve in office. Second, laws that govern everyone should be justified through reasons accessible to citizens who do not share the sponsoring faith. Third, religious accommodations should be evaluated not only by the burden on the believer but also by the burden shifted onto others.

A simpler test may also help: would the group accept the same power being exercised by a different religion? Would Christians support mandatory classroom prayer if the prayer were Islamic? Would advocates of biblical curriculum accept state-selected readings from the Qur’an, the Vedas, or secular humanist texts? Would a religious employer accept an atheist employer denying benefits because of a deeply held moral objection to religion? If a political power appears legitimate only when one’s own faith controls it, the principle is not religious liberty. It is religious advantage.

This standard will not resolve every dispute. Public reasons can be contested. Harms can be difficult to measure. Rights can collide. But the standard keeps the focus where it belongs: not on whether religious people are allowed to participate, but on whether participation preserves equal citizenship.

Some critics may argue that requiring public justification unfairly privileges secular reasoning because religious reasons are excluded from legal legitimacy. But public justification does not require citizens to erase their faith. It asks them to translate coercive policy into terms that others can examine without first converting. A believer may begin with scripture and still offer arguments about public welfare, human dignity, medical evidence, social consequences, and rights. Translation is not betrayal. It is the work of sharing power with people who do not share the same source of authority.

That shared power is the heart of democracy. Democracy is not a system in which every citizen gets laws matching their deepest beliefs. It is a system in which people with conflicting beliefs govern together without making one another politically subordinate. It requires all groups to accept limits. Religious citizens must accept that not every sin should be a crime. Secular citizens must accept that religious moral arguments belong in public debate. Majorities must accept that winning elections does not grant ownership of conscience. Minorities must accept that disagreement is not automatically persecution.

The distinction between disagreement and persecution is especially important. A Christian may feel criticized, culturally displaced, or politically defeated without being persecuted. Losing a policy debate is not the same as losing religious freedom. Being required to follow a neutral law is not necessarily anti-Christian. Encountering public recognition of other faiths, families, or moral traditions is not erasure. Equality can feel like loss when privilege has long felt normal.

At the same time, religious communities can face genuine discrimination, and dismissing every religious-liberty claim as disguised bigotry would be intellectually lazy and morally dangerous. Minority faiths, unpopular sects, prisoners, workers, immigrants, and students may require protection from laws or institutions built around majority assumptions. The answer is not skepticism toward religion itself. The answer is careful attention to power, context, and third-party harm.

The same religious-liberty principle that protects a Christian employee’s Sabbath may protect a Muslim employee’s prayer schedule, a Jewish student’s holy days, a Sikh’s articles of faith, or an atheist’s right not to participate in prayer. The principle becomes credible when it protects everyone consistently. It becomes suspicious when it appears primarily as a tool for preserving the dominant religion’s control over others.

Religious participation and religious domination can therefore be distinguished by the direction of power. Participation asks for a voice. Domination demands final authority. Participation enters debate. Domination closes debate by invoking divine command. Participation seeks freedom to live according to conscience. Domination seeks power to make others live according to that conscience. Participation accepts equal citizenship. Domination divides the public into moral owners and tolerated guests.

This boundary does not ask religious people to become less religious. It asks them to become more democratic. It asks them to trust persuasion rather than compulsion, witness rather than enforcement, service rather than ownership. For Christianity specifically, this boundary may also be spiritually healthier. A faith dependent on state power risks replacing conversion with compliance and moral transformation with legal fear. The state can force behavior. It cannot produce belief, repentance, love, or grace. Police power is many things, but it is a terrible evangelist.

The counterargument therefore strengthens rather than weakens the article’s thesis. Religion can be a vital democratic force. Religious conviction can challenge injustice, form conscience, and inspire sacrifice. The answer is not to silence faith. It is to preserve the distinction between faith speaking and faith ruling.

Religious participation is democratic when it joins the common argument as one voice among many. Religious domination begins when that voice claims divine entitlement to the final word. The difference is not whether religion enters politics. It is whether everyone else is still free when it arrives.


# Conclusion: Protecting Faith by Limiting Power

Christianity has shaped the United States and much of the wider world in ways too profound to dismiss and too contradictory to romanticize. It has given people language for mercy, justice, sacrifice, forgiveness, dignity, and moral courage. It has inspired abolitionists, civil-rights organizers, caregivers, educators, reformers, prisoners of conscience, and ordinary people who chose compassion when cruelty would have been easier. It has helped build hospitals, sustain communities, comfort the grieving, feed the hungry, shelter the displaced, and challenge governments that treated human beings as disposable.

Christianity has also been used to justify conquest, slavery, segregation, patriarchy, censorship, forced assimilation, anti-LGBTQ persecution, reproductive control, suppression of inquiry, national hierarchy, and political violence. It has blessed both liberation and domination. It has stood beside the oppressed and, at other moments, stood much too comfortably beside the throne. That contradiction cannot be explained away as a few unfortunate exceptions committed by people who simply misunderstood the faith. It is part of the historical record, and any serious examination of Christianity’s political influence must look directly at both sides of it.

The central question of this article has therefore never been whether Christianity is good or bad. A religious tradition spanning centuries, continents, cultures, denominations, languages, and political systems cannot be reduced to a single moral verdict without flattening both history and human experience. The more important question is what happens when Christianity moves from faith into authority, from persuasion into compulsion, and from conscience into law.

That movement changes the moral character of religious influence. Faith may ask, persuade, inspire, warn, comfort, challenge, and invite. Law commands. Law regulates, licenses, funds, restricts, punishes, and compels. A church may define sin for its members. A government defines legal consequences for everyone. When religious doctrine acquires the force of the state, people outside the faith can no longer simply disagree, leave the congregation, reject the teaching, or follow another conscience. The exit disappears.

This is why the distinction between religious participation and religious domination is the foundation of the argument. Religious participation belongs in democracy. Religious citizens have every right to vote, organize, advocate, protest, preach, publish, serve in office, and allow faith to shape their moral priorities. A democracy that excluded religious conviction would not be pluralistic. It would merely replace religious domination with secular domination and congratulate itself on the wardrobe change.

Religious domination is different. It begins when one community’s faith is treated as the nation’s rightful identity, when its doctrines become the legal obligations of outsiders, when its discomfort becomes a reason to restrict another person’s freedom, or when its claim to divine truth is used to close public debate. Participation asks for a voice. Domination demands the final word.

That distinction also explains why religious influence has produced such different outcomes throughout American history. Christian conviction helped animate abolition and civil rights, but Christian language also defended slavery and segregation. The difference was not simply that one side used religion and the other did not. Both did. The difference was what their religious arguments asked power to do.

The abolitionist and civil-rights traditions used religious language to widen the circle of citizenship. They called the nation to honor dignity it had denied and promises it had broken. They connected faith with constitutional principle, human experience, public evidence, and rights that could be claimed by believers and nonbelievers alike. Their vision did not require every citizen to become Christian before deserving freedom.

Religious domination moves in the opposite direction. It uses sacred authority to narrow belonging, regulate outsiders, preserve hierarchy, or make one group’s moral certainty the legal burden of everyone else. It does not widen citizenship. It places conditions on it.

That difference is the moral compass this debate requires. Religious politics should not be judged merely by whether it invokes God, scripture, tradition, family, morality, or religious liberty. Those words can carry compassion or camouflage. The better question is always what direction the power moves. Does it protect people’s freedom to live according to conscience, or does it make one conscience authoritative over others? Does it widen the circle of citizenship, or redraw the boundary around a favored group? Does it challenge power, or sanctify it?

This question becomes especially urgent when religious authority reaches into bodies, families, schools, medicine, identity, and knowledge. These are the places where political power becomes intimate.

A reproductive law shaped by one theological account of personhood does not remain in a legislative chamber. It enters pregnancies, miscarriages, emergency rooms, marriages, travel plans, medical judgments, and grief. A policy shaped by religious ideas about sexuality or gender does not remain an abstract statement of values. It enters classrooms, libraries, healthcare, housing, employment, families, and the private calculations people make about whether it is safe to tell the truth about themselves.

A religiously framed curriculum does not merely add one more text to a reading list. It teaches children whose tradition counts as heritage and whose requires explanation. A conversion effort does not merely express doctrinal disagreement. It may teach a young person that love, shelter, family, community, and salvation depend on becoming someone else. A restriction on scientific research does not merely express ethical concern. It can determine which questions receive funding, which treatments are developed, and which patients are told to keep waiting while theology and evidence argue over the future.

These consequences reveal why sincerity is not enough. Religious people may sincerely believe they are protecting life, family, children, morality, truth, or civilization. Sincerity matters when assessing character, but it cannot be the final measure of justice. A sincere law can still injure. A sincere parent can still reject a child. A sincere institution can still discriminate. A sincere majority can still become oppressive.

Power must be judged by its effects as well as its intentions.

This is one of the clearest lessons of selective literalism. Religious movements often present their political priorities as unavoidable expressions of scripture, yet interpretation is already happening everywhere. Modern context is considered when churches address divorce, abuse, remarriage, wealth, war, slavery, economic life, and countless other matters. Historical circumstances are examined. Ancient languages are debated. Pastoral judgment is exercised. Exceptions are recognized. Mercy is discovered.

But when the subject becomes sexuality, gender, reproduction, or nontraditional family life, some of those same movements suddenly insist that interpretation is impossible, context is corruption, and the text leaves no room for anything but enforcement. That is not the absence of interpretation. It is interpretation selectively denied to the people who most need its compassion.

If historical context, pastoral care, human suffering, translation, and modern knowledge may shape the application of one teaching, then intellectual honesty requires that they be considered across the tradition. A religious community may still reach conservative conclusions. It may preach them, practice them, and defend them. But it should admit that it has interpreted, chosen, prioritized, and applied. “The Bible says” cannot become a trapdoor through which responsibility disappears.

The problem becomes even clearer when Christianity is used performatively. Political leaders do not need to embody Christian ethics if Christian symbols alone can provide legitimacy. They need only hold the Bible, invoke God, promise to defend Christians, condemn the correct enemies, and perform cultural alignment. The symbol replaces the substance.

In that environment, mercy becomes optional. Humility becomes weakness. Care for the poor becomes charity branding rather than public obligation. Truth becomes negotiable when a lie protects the movement. Repentance becomes unnecessary because the enemy is always worse. Christianity is not merely misrepresented to outsiders; it is diluted for believers themselves.

A demanding faith becomes easier, cheaper, and more politically useful. It is stripped down into slogans, symbols, grievances, and enemies. The political movement gains religious legitimacy, while Christianity loses moral depth. That is a terrible bargain dressed as victory.

Christian nationalism takes this performance further by treating Christianity not only as a moral influence but as a claim of national ownership. It imagines the United States as properly Christian and casts pluralism as decline, displacement, or theft. Non-Christians and dissenting Christians may remain citizens, but their belonging becomes conditional. They are present in the country without being centered in its sacred story.

This is why Christian nationalism threatens both democracy and Christianity. It threatens democracy by weakening equal citizenship and treating religious identity as evidence of national legitimacy. It threatens Christianity by fusing faith with grievance, dominance, and political control. A tradition that once called believers to love enemies can become a movement that requires enemies to maintain its sense of purpose.

That is not strength. It is dependence.

If Christianity must dominate others to prove itself, then its political victory has already become a spiritual defeat.

The global comparison makes the pattern harder to dismiss. Iran and Afghanistan show what happens when religious authority captures the state. China shows what happens when the state captures religion. India demonstrates the danger of majority faith becoming national identity. Myanmar reveals how religion, ethnicity, and nationalism can help mark an entire people for exclusion, displacement, and genocidal violence. Israel and Palestine show how sacred history, territory, security, trauma, and unequal legal power can become entangled in ways that make one population’s protection another population’s confinement.

These cases are not identical. They differ in theology, history, severity, law, culture, and political structure. They should not be flattened into moral equivalents. Their value lies in revealing recurring mechanisms.

A religious or ideological identity becomes a claim to ownership. Ownership becomes privilege. Privilege becomes authority. Authority becomes coercion. Outsiders become threats. Dissent becomes disloyalty. Harm becomes protection. Domination becomes order.

Sometimes government claims to speak for God. Sometimes government claims authority over God. Liberty contracts either way.

China is particularly important because it exposes the deeper argument beneath the religious examples. The ultimate danger is not simply religion. The deeper danger is concentrated power wrapped in unquestionable moral certainty. An officially atheist government can surveil worship, edit sermons, recognize approved religious leaders, close churches, imprison believers, and restructure faith according to party ideology. Secular authoritarianism is not rescued by the absence of theology. The owner of conscience has merely changed uniforms.

The principle must therefore remain universal. Christian domination is wrong. Islamic theocracy is wrong. Hindu nationalism that makes minorities conditional citizens is wrong. Buddhist nationalism used to mark Muslims as foreign or dangerous is wrong. Jewish religious nationalism used to justify permanent inequality or territorial domination is wrong. An atheist state that imprisons, supervises, edits, or eliminates independent religious life is also wrong.

The objection cannot depend on whether the dominant worldview feels familiar.

That is also why the strongest counterargument ultimately reinforces this article’s thesis. Religious people have the right to participate fully in public life. Religious language has helped movements expose injustice, form conscience, and call society toward moral responsibility. The answer is not to silence faith, ban religious reasoning, or pretend secular citizens enter politics without comprehensive moral beliefs of their own.

The answer is reciprocity, public justification, equal citizenship, and restraint.

Would Christians accept mandatory classroom prayer if the prayer were Islamic? Would supporters of state-approved Bible readings accept required lessons from the Qur’an, the Vedas, or secular humanist philosophy? Would a Christian employee accept an atheist employer denying benefits because of a deeply held moral objection to religion? Would a Christian majority accept another faith controlling marriage, education, reproduction, public morality, and national identity?

If a political power appears legitimate only when one’s own faith controls it, the principle is not religious liberty. It is religious advantage.

This reciprocity test is not perfect, but it exposes a great deal. People often recognize coercion more clearly when imagining themselves beneath someone else’s authority. The arrangement that feels like heritage when one’s own tradition controls it may feel very different when another tradition writes the prayer, chooses the scripture, defines the family, regulates the body, and decides which citizens belong naturally.

Religious freedom means little if it protects only the religion currently holding power.

The same principle applies to compromise. Democratic restraint can be painful. Believers may sincerely feel that society permits conduct they consider deeply wrong. Secular and minority citizens may sincerely fear that religious accommodations legitimize ideas that threaten their rights or dignity. These experiences should not be mocked or dismissed.

But they are not always equivalent.

Losing the power to control another person is not the same as losing control over one’s own life. Being unable to make a neighbor obey a religious teaching is not the same as being forbidden to follow that teaching oneself. Moral discomfort caused by someone else’s freedom is not equivalent to losing bodily autonomy, family recognition, medical access, educational inclusion, housing, safety, or equal protection.

The discomfort of pluralism can be genuine without becoming a constitutional injury.

A democracy asks citizens to live beside choices they may condemn and beliefs they may consider false, harmful, or even offensive. That mutual frustration is not a defect in pluralism. It is the cost of sharing a political community with people who do not answer to the same moral authority.

The line is crossed when one group treats its distress as sufficient reason to reduce another group’s freedom. At that point, compromise stops being mutual. One side retains its beliefs and practices; the other side loses choices, rights, safety, or recognition. The language may remain “balance,” but the burden has become distinctly one-directional.

The pain of not controlling one’s neighbors is not the same as the harm of being controlled by them.

A workable democratic boundary follows from all of this. Religious citizens must remain free to believe, worship, organize, persuade, vote, advocate, protest, and serve in office. Laws that govern everyone should be justified through public reasons that citizens can evaluate without first accepting one religious authority. Religious accommodations should be assessed not only by the burden placed on the believer, but also by the burden transferred to workers, students, patients, children, customers, families, and other citizens.

This standard will not solve every conflict. Rights collide. Harms can be disputed. Evidence may be incomplete. Religious communities may disagree internally, and secular institutions can be biased, careless, or coercive. There will be no perfectly neutral arrangement because law always reflects moral judgments.

But neutrality does not require emptiness. It requires fairness in how power is distributed and restrained. A government may protect religious exercise without adopting religious authority. It may accommodate conscience without making outsiders pay the full cost. It may acknowledge Christianity’s historical influence without defining the nation as Christian property. It may allow faith to speak without granting faith an unquestionable veto.

The boundary is simple even when its application is difficult: faith may guide the believer, but it should not govern the unbeliever merely because it claims divine certainty.

This limit does not diminish faith. It may protect faith from becoming something smaller than itself.

Christianity does not need police power to preach mercy. It does not need a legislature to practice compassion. It does not need a school board to teach humility, a court order to feed the hungry, or a criminal statute to love the stranger. The most morally powerful forms of Christianity have rarely depended on domination. They have depended on witness, sacrifice, service, courage, repentance, and solidarity.

The state can force behavior. It cannot create belief. It cannot compel grace, manufacture love, produce repentance, or turn legal compliance into spiritual transformation. Police power is many things, but it remains a terrible evangelist.

When Christianity reaches for government control, it may gain the ability to regulate conduct while losing the ability to persuade conscience. People may comply with the law while resenting the faith associated with it. Outsiders may come to know Christianity primarily through restriction, condemnation, and fear. Believers may confuse political victory with spiritual health. The church may gain access to the throne while forgetting why it once stood with people crushed beneath it.

Limiting religious authority is therefore not only a protection for nonbelievers and minorities. It can also be a protection for religion itself. It keeps the state from deciding which theology is official, which believers are authentic, which doctrines are acceptable, and which forms of worship deserve protection. It prevents Christianity from becoming dependent on political power and prevents political power from hollowing Christianity into a campaign brand.

The wall between faith and state is not merely a barrier. It is breathing room.

It gives religion space to remain voluntary, contested, diverse, morally demanding, and free. It gives citizens room to believe, doubt, convert, leave, return, reinterpret, dissent, or belong nowhere at all. It allows people of different faiths and no faith to share law without being forced to share theology.

That is the democratic promise worth defending.

The United States does not need a public life emptied of religion. It needs a public life where no religion owns the government. It does not need citizens without conviction. It needs institutions strong enough to prevent conviction from becoming domination. It does not need Christianity to disappear. It needs Christianity, like every faith and ideology, to live within boundaries that protect the conscience of everyone.

Faith may speak.

It may persuade.

It may organize, protest, comfort, challenge, mourn, celebrate, serve, and demand justice.

It may call the powerful to account and stand beside those power has abandoned.

But it should not rule unwilling citizens by force.

When faith becomes law, it risks losing its soul while taking freedom with it. When faith accepts democratic limits, it does not become weaker. It becomes capable of offering moral witness without coercion, conviction without domination, and community without ownership.

That is not hostility toward religion. It is respect for conscience.

It is not the removal of faith from public life. It is the refusal to turn faith into a government weapon.

And it is not merely a compromise between believers and nonbelievers. It is the condition under which both can remain free.

The question was never whether Christianity has shaped America.

It has.

The question was never whether Christians should participate in public life.

They should.

The question is whether any religion should possess the authority to govern people who do not share it.

A pluralistic democracy must answer no.

Not because faith is dangerous.

But because power is.

Every generation must answer the same question: will we trust freedom enough to allow persuasion, or will we trust power enough to replace it?

History has already shown what happens when governments decide they speak for God—or when they decide they can speak instead of Him.

The lesson is not that faith has no place in public life. The lesson is that no government should ever mistake itself for the voice of God.
