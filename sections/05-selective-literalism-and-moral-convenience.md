# Selective Literalism and Moral Convenience

One of the clearest signs that politicized Christianity often functions less as consistent theology and more as political convenience is the selective way biblical authority is applied. Divorce provides a useful example. The New Testament contains strict teachings on divorce, particularly in passages attributed to Jesus, where divorce and remarriage are treated as morally serious and, in some interpretations, deeply restricted. Yet in many modern Christian communities, divorce has been interpreted through a more flexible and compassionate lens. It is often treated as unfortunate, painful, or forgivable rather than as a permanent moral disqualification.

This pastoral flexibility is not inherently wrong. In fact, it may reflect a humane recognition of abuse, abandonment, incompatibility, and human suffering. The issue is that the same interpretive generosity is not always extended to other areas of public morality. Questions involving sexuality, gender identity, reproductive rights, or family structure are often treated by politicized religious movements as fixed, timeless, and legally enforceable.

In this way, biblical interpretation becomes selective: modern context is allowed when it protects the comfort of the dominant group, but rejected when it would protect the freedom or dignity of marginalized people. The problem is not that religious communities interpret scripture in changing social contexts; the problem is pretending they do not do so while selectively denying that same interpretive flexibility to others.

This selective literalism becomes politically dangerous because it disguises choice as inevitability. A community may choose to read one passage through compassion, history, trauma, and modern circumstance, while reading another as rigid and universal. That is interpretation, not pure obedience. But when religious movements deny that interpretive process, they can present their political priorities as timeless divine mandates. The result is moral convenience dressed as conviction, and the outfit is not as flattering as it thinks.
