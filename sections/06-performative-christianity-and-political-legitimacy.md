# Performative Christianity and Political Legitimacy

Performative Christianity is not simply hypocrisy, although hypocrisy is certainly one of its more enthusiastic interns. It is the use of Christian language, symbols, and identity markers to claim moral legitimacy without submitting to the ethical demands those symbols are supposed to carry. In public life, Christianity can become less a faith tradition than a political costume: a way to signal belonging, reassure a base, attack opponents, and present power as righteousness. The performance works because Christian symbols still carry cultural authority in the United States. A Bible, a prayer, a church backdrop, a reference to “faith,” “family,” or “Judeo-Christian values” can make a political agenda appear morally serious before anyone has examined what that agenda actually does.

This is not the same as sincere religious expression. Many public officials are genuinely religious. Many voters are shaped by faith in honest, serious, and morally demanding ways. Religious language in politics is not automatically performative, and a democratic society should not treat it as suspect merely because it is religious. The problem begins when Christian identity becomes a substitute for Christian ethics. When the symbol matters more than the substance, when the language of faith is used to bless cruelty, and when religious affiliation becomes a loyalty badge rather than a moral discipline, Christianity is no longer guiding political life. It is being used as a prop.

That prop function is especially visible when politicians invoke Christianity while behaving in ways that would be difficult to reconcile with the ethical teachings they claim to defend. A leader may speak of faith while practicing dishonesty, contempt, vengeance, greed, scapegoating, or indifference to the vulnerable. A movement may describe itself as Christian while supporting policies that punish the poor, demonize migrants, mock weakness, excuse corruption, or treat cruelty as strength. In these cases, Christianity is not absent from politics; it is present as branding. The faith becomes a logo printed on a product that may contain none of the advertised ingredients.

One of the clearest modern examples of sacred symbolism as political theater was the Bible photo opportunity outside St. John’s Church in June 2020. Then-President Donald Trump stood outside the church holding a Bible after leaving the White House during nationwide protests following the murder of George Floyd [@ap_stjohns_photo_op_probe]. The image was powerful precisely because it was religiously staged: a president, a church, a Bible, cameras, and a national crisis. But the moral meaning of the symbol was deeply contested. The issue was not simply that a political leader held a Bible. The issue was that a sacred text appeared to be used as a visual instrument of authority, order, and domination rather than humility, repentance, or care for the wounded. It was less sermon than set design.

That episode matters because performative Christianity often depends on visual shorthand. It does not require careful theology. It requires recognizable signals. The Bible does not need to be opened. The prayer does not need to transform conduct. The church does not need to shape policy through mercy or justice. The symbol only needs to communicate alignment: this leader is on the side of “faith,” and therefore criticism can be framed as hostility to religion itself. The performance turns religious imagery into political armor. Once the armor is on, accountability becomes easier to deflect.

This is why the gap between religious image and religious conduct matters. When Christian language becomes a shield for political behavior, it can make moral criticism seem illegitimate. A leader can frame opposition as anti-Christian, anti-family, anti-tradition, or anti-American. A policy can be defended not because it is just, effective, or humane, but because it is said to represent religious values. In that environment, the content of the policy may matter less than the identity it performs. The question becomes not “Does this reflect mercy, justice, humility, truth, or care for the least of these?” but “Does this sound like it belongs to our side?” That is not moral reasoning. That is team merchandise with incense.

Performative Christianity also narrows the public definition of what counts as Christian. In American politics, Christianity is often reduced to a small set of symbolic issues: abortion, sexuality, gender, school prayer, religious displays, and opposition to secularism. These issues may matter deeply to many Christians, but they do not exhaust the tradition. A Christianity reduced to identity markers loses sight of teachings about poverty, truthfulness, peacemaking, forgiveness, humility, hospitality, greed, and care for strangers. It becomes possible for political leaders to appear “Christian” by performing the correct cultural signals while ignoring the harder ethical demands of the faith. The result is a version of Christianity that asks very little of power and very much of everyone power wants to control.

This narrowing is politically useful because it allows religious identity to be measured by affiliation rather than practice. A politician can be treated as a defender of Christianity even if many citizens do not view that politician as personally religious. Pew Research Center reported in 2026 that seven-in-ten Americans said Donald Trump was not too religious or not at all religious [@pew_trump_religiosity_2026]. That finding is revealing not because one politician’s personal faith can be measured perfectly from the outside, but because it shows how political religious legitimacy may not depend on perceived piety. A leader may be seen as religiously useful even if not seen as religiously devout. In performative politics, usefulness can matter more than holiness. The bar is not “Christlike.” The bar is “will fight for our side.” Conveniently low, but apparently very sturdy.

This dynamic does serious damage to democratic life. When Christianity is used as a legitimacy machine, political disagreement can be recoded as spiritual betrayal. Opponents are not merely wrong; they are godless, demonic, anti-family, anti-Christian, or enemies of the nation’s moral order. That shift raises the emotional stakes of politics and lowers the possibility of compromise. If one side is imagined as defending God and the other as opposing God, ordinary democratic negotiation starts to look like moral surrender. Debate becomes warfare. Policy becomes testimony. Elections become altar calls with better polling data and worse music.

Performative Christianity also damages Christianity itself. When religious symbols are repeatedly attached to cruelty, corruption, dishonesty, or domination, the public meaning of the faith changes. People outside the church may come to associate Christianity not with mercy, humility, forgiveness, or love of neighbor, but with control, grievance, exclusion, and political appetite. That is not persecution. That is brand damage, and the call may be coming from inside the sanctuary. A faith tradition can survive criticism from outsiders. It has a harder time surviving being reduced by its supposed defenders to a campaign aesthetic.

Performative Christianity does not merely misrepresent Christianity to outsiders; it can dilute Christianity for believers themselves. When faith is repeatedly reduced to political identity, culture-war signaling, and loyalty tests, the deeper demands of the tradition become easier to ignore. Mercy becomes optional. Humility becomes weakness. Care for the poor becomes charity branding rather than moral obligation. Truth becomes negotiable if the lie protects the movement. Repentance becomes unnecessary because the enemy is always worse. In that sense, performative Christianity does not defend Christianity so much as thin it out, stripping a demanding faith down into a set of slogans, symbols, and enemies.

The danger is especially sharp because performative Christianity often claims to be protecting religion. It presents itself as a defense of faith against secular hostility, moral decline, or cultural displacement. Sometimes those fears are sincerely felt. Many religious Americans do believe that religion’s influence is changing, and Pew has found that Americans remain divided over religion’s role in public life and government [@pew_religion_public_life_2026]. But fear of cultural change can make symbolic politics more attractive. When people feel their religious world slipping, a leader who performs strength on behalf of Christianity may feel like protection, even if the performance empties the faith of its ethical demands.

That is the central danger: performative Christianity can train people to confuse dominance with faithfulness. It suggests that Christianity is strongest when it wins elections, controls courts, commands schools, shapes law, and punishes dissent. But Christian moral credibility has rarely come from domination. It has come from witness, sacrifice, compassion, courage, and the willingness to stand with the vulnerable even when doing so costs something. When Christianity is performed mainly as power, it risks losing the very qualities that made it morally compelling in the first place.

A democratic society does not need public officials to hide their faith. It needs them to stop using faith as camouflage for power. Religious language may inspire moral seriousness, but it should also invite moral scrutiny. If a politician invokes Christianity, the fair question is not simply whether the words sound religious. The fair question is whether the politics reflect the ethical burden those words carry. Does the invocation of faith lead to mercy or punishment? Truth or manipulation? Humility or domination? Solidarity with the vulnerable or protection for the powerful?

Performative Christianity becomes dangerous when it turns those questions into disloyalty. It asks citizens to honor the symbol without examining the conduct. It treats religious language as proof of moral authority rather than a claim that must be tested. But faith that cannot survive scrutiny is not being protected; it is being managed. Christianity does not become stronger when politicians hold it up for cameras. It becomes stronger when its ethical demands are taken seriously, especially by those who claim it most loudly.

The issue, then, is not whether Christianity may appear in public life. It may. The issue is whether Christianity is being practiced as conscience or performed as legitimacy. A faith used to humble power is very different from a faith used to sanctify power. One can challenge injustice. The other can baptize it. Performative Christianity is dangerous because it allows political authority to borrow the language of holiness while avoiding the discipline of morality. It lets power dress itself in sacred symbols and then act offended when anyone notices the costume does not fit.
