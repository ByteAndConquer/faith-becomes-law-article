# Performative Christianity and Political Legitimacy

A major issue in modern American politics is not simply that Christianity influences public debate, but that religious language is often used performatively by political leaders who do not consistently follow the moral teachings they claim to defend. In this context, Christianity becomes less a faith tradition and more a political symbol: a way to signal loyalty, claim moral authority, and divide citizens into insiders and outsiders.

This form of politicized Christianity often focuses less on compassion, humility, justice, or care for the vulnerable, and more on controlling laws related to sexuality, gender, education, reproduction, and public identity. When religion is used this way, it no longer functions primarily as spiritual guidance; it becomes a tool for power.

Performative Christianity works because religious language carries moral weight. It can make a political program appear righteous before its substance has been examined. It can allow leaders to claim the authority of faith while avoiding the ethical burden of practicing it. The performance becomes a kind of public credential: a way of saying “trust me” without submitting to the moral demands that trust should require.

This is why the gap between religious language and political behavior matters. When Christian symbols are used to bless cruelty, excuse corruption, or frame opponents as enemies of God, Christianity is not merely participating in politics. It is being repurposed as political theater. The danger is not only hypocrisy, although there is plenty of that buffet to go around. The deeper danger is that performative religion can train citizens to confuse religious branding with moral seriousness.
