# Politicized Evangelicalism and Christian Nationalism

Politicized evangelicalism and Christian nationalism intensify the movement from faith as conscience to faith as political authority. They do not simply argue that Christians should participate in public life. That would be ordinary democratic participation. Instead, they often suggest that the nation itself is properly Christian, that Christian identity should shape public legitimacy, and that political disagreement with a conservative Christian agenda reflects moral, spiritual, or national decline. In this framework, Christianity is not merely a faith tradition. It becomes a claim about who the country belongs to.

This distinction matters because evangelical Christianity is not inherently nationalist, authoritarian, or politically coercive. Evangelical faith can be deeply personal, missionary, charitable, communal, and morally serious. It has also taken diverse political forms across history and across racial, regional, and denominational communities. The problem is not evangelical belief itself. The problem is politicized evangelicalism: the use of evangelical identity, language, networks, fears, and moral authority to build political power. That distinction is necessary because critique should be aimed at domination, not at sincere belief. The target is not Christians voting their conscience. The target is Christianity being turned into a citizenship test.

Christian nationalism goes further by tying Christianity to national identity. It suggests that the United States was founded as, or should become, a distinctly Christian nation whose laws, institutions, and public culture should reflect Christian authority. Pew Research Center found in 2022 that 45 percent of U.S. adults said the United States should be a “Christian nation,” although Americans meant different things by that phrase and many still supported church-state separation [@pew_christian_nation_2022]. That ambiguity matters. For some people, “Christian nation” may mean cultural heritage, moral aspiration, or recognition of Christianity’s historical influence. For others, it means a political order in which Christianity receives official privilege. The phrase sounds simple until someone asks who gets to define “Christian,” who gets protected by it, and who gets governed by it.

The constitutional problem is immediate. The First Amendment rejects religious establishment while protecting free exercise [@archives_bill_of_rights]. That means Christianity may influence citizens, communities, and moral debate, but the government may not become the instrument of one faith. Christian nationalism strains that boundary because it imagines national belonging through religious identity. It does not merely say Christians have rights within the nation. It implies that Christianity has a rightful claim over the nation. That is a very different proposition. One protects conscience. The other assigns ownership.

This ownership logic is why Christian nationalism is dangerous to pluralism. A pluralistic democracy assumes that people of many religions and no religion belong equally to the political community. Christian nationalism unsettles that equality by treating Christianity as the default identity of the nation and everyone else as guests, exceptions, threats, or tolerated outsiders. Non-Christians may still be legally present, but their belonging becomes conditional. They are welcome as long as they do not challenge the dominant religious story. That is not equal citizenship. That is hospitality with a house rule: remember who owns the place.

Recent survey data shows that these ideas are not fringe curiosities hiding in the political attic. The Public Religion Research Institute (PRRI) found in its 2025 American Values Atlas, published in 2026, that 11 percent of Americans qualified as Christian nationalism Adherents and 21 percent as Sympathizers [@prri_mapping_christian_nationalism_2026]. The same research found that a majority of Republicans qualified as either Adherents or Sympathizers, and that white evangelical Protestants and Hispanic Protestants were the only major religious groups in which a majority qualified as Christian nationalism supporters [@prri_mapping_christian_nationalism_2026]. These numbers do not mean every Christian is a Christian nationalist, or that every evangelical supports the same political program. They do show that Christian nationalism is a measurable force in American political life, not merely a label people throw around when the discourse gets bored and wants a sharper object.

This is also why Christian nationalism should not be confused with Christianity itself. The difference matters morally and analytically. Christianity is a broad, internally diverse religious tradition with many theologies, histories, cultures, and political expressions. Christian nationalism is a political ideology that fuses Christianity with national identity and state power. It often treats one version of Christianity as the moral core of the nation and frames political opposition as rebellion against that sacred order. In that move, faith becomes less a path of discipleship and more a border wall around belonging.

The emotional power of Christian nationalism comes from loss. Many Americans who support religious nationalism believe the country has drifted away from its proper moral foundation. They may experience pluralism not as democratic equality, but as displacement. Secularism feels like hostility. Religious diversity feels like decline. LGBTQIA+ rights, reproductive autonomy, immigration, racial justice, and changing gender roles may be interpreted as evidence that the nation has abandoned God. In that atmosphere, politics becomes restoration. The goal is not merely to pass policies; it is to take the country back. Back from whom is the question that tends to make the room go quiet.

That restoration narrative is powerful because it transforms political desire into sacred duty. If the nation is imagined as Christian property, then efforts to preserve Christian dominance can be framed as defense rather than aggression. Laws restricting abortion, LGBTQIA+ rights, classroom discussion, school materials, or public recognition of minority identities can be described as protecting children, restoring morality, or defending religious freedom. The language is soothing. The effect is not. When one group’s restoration requires another group’s restriction, the politics of protection becomes the politics of control wearing a sweater vest.

Christian nationalism also changes how opponents are understood. In ordinary democracy, political opponents are fellow citizens with different views. In religious-national politics, opponents can become enemies of God, enemies of the family, enemies of the nation, or agents of moral corruption. This is where disagreement becomes spiritualized. A debate about curriculum becomes a battle over innocence. A debate about abortion becomes a battle between life and evil. A debate about LGBTQIA+ rights becomes a battle over civilization itself. Once politics is framed this way, compromise becomes betrayal. You do not negotiate with demons. You defeat them. That is a terrible foundation for democratic life, unless the goal is to turn every school board meeting into a tiny apocalypse.

This dynamic helps explain why Christian nationalism often overlaps with performative Christianity. The public performance of faith supplies the symbols; Christian nationalism supplies the political story. The Bible, the flag, the church, the family, the soldier, the child, and the nation are woven into a single moral image. That image is emotionally potent because it collapses several loyalties into one. To criticize the political agenda is made to look like an attack on faith, family, patriotism, and innocence all at once. That is not an argument. It is a hostage situation with better graphic design.

The danger grows when this fusion of Christianity and nation enters law. Religious citizens may advocate for policies shaped by their beliefs, but Christian nationalism seeks more than participation. It seeks alignment between government and a particular religious identity. The state begins to reflect one faith’s symbols, one faith’s moral priorities, and one faith’s story of national purpose. Public schools become stages for religious display. Courts become arenas for theological claims dressed as constitutional reasoning. Legislatures become tools for enforcing moral boundaries that many citizens do not share. The state does not need to declare an official church to behave like it has a favorite.

This is especially harmful to Christians who do not share the nationalist vision. Progressive Christians, Black church traditions, mainline Protestants, Catholics with different political commitments, queer Christians, immigrant churches, peace churches, and others may find their faith publicly overwritten by a louder political version of Christianity. Christian nationalism often speaks as if it represents “the Christians,” when in reality it represents a contested political theology within Christianity. That flattening is itself a form of religious harm. It steals the microphone from the diversity of Christian witness and hands it to the faction most willing to confuse volume with authority.

It also harms non-Christians and the religiously unaffiliated by making them politically suspect. Jews, Muslims, Hindus, Buddhists, Sikhs, atheists, agnostics, and others may hear Christian nationalist rhetoric not as harmless heritage language, but as a warning about their place in the civic order. If the nation is Christian, then they are something else in relation to the nation: outsiders, exceptions, tolerated minorities, or people whose equality depends on the generosity of the dominant faith. That is not religious liberty. It is majority comfort with minority vulnerability.

Christian nationalism’s defenders often argue that critics are simply attacking Christians for having political views. That objection deserves to be answered directly. Christians have every right to political views. They have every right to organize, vote, protest, run for office, advocate, and argue from moral conviction. The issue is not Christian participation. The issue is Christian supremacy. There is a difference between saying “my faith shapes my politics” and saying “my faith should define the nation’s laws and identity.” The first is democracy. The second is domination.

That distinction is the backbone of this article’s broader claim. Religious participation is democratic; religious domination is not. Christianity may inspire citizens to pursue justice, mercy, community, and reform. It may challenge public cruelty and call the nation to moral accountability. But when Christianity is fused with nationalism, it becomes less able to challenge power because it has begun to identify with power. It no longer stands outside the state asking whether the state is just. It stands beside the state asking whether the state is Christian enough. That is a dangerous trade.

Christian nationalism therefore threatens both democracy and Christianity. It threatens democracy by weakening equal citizenship and treating religious identity as a measure of national belonging. It threatens Christianity by reducing faith to political identity, cultural grievance, and control over outsiders. A faith that once called believers to love enemies can become a movement that needs enemies in order to feel alive. That is not strength. That is dependence. If Christianity must dominate others to prove itself, then its political victory has already become a spiritual failure.

This is not merely a future danger or a theoretical concern. The movement of Christian identity into public authority is already visible in fights over schools, curriculum, and religious display. In 2026, a federal appeals court ruled that Texas could require public schools to display the Ten Commandments in classrooms, a decision critics argued imposed specific religious views through government authority [@ap_texas_ten_commandments_2026]. Texas also approved a required K–12 reading list that includes Bible passages and stories beginning in the 2030–31 school year, following broader efforts to place Christian texts and symbols inside public education [@reuters_texas_bible_reading_2026; @texastribune_bible_curriculum_2026]. These developments do not mean the United States has become a theocracy, and the argument should not be made carelessly. But they do show how Christian nationalist assumptions can move from rhetoric into institutions: first as heritage, then as curriculum, then as legal requirement. The line between “recognizing influence” and “normalizing authority” is already being tested.

The proper answer is not to drive Christians out of politics. It is to refuse the idea that Christianity owns the nation. Christians may participate fully in democracy, but they must do so as citizens among citizens, not as landlords of the republic. The United States can acknowledge Christianity’s historical influence without surrendering public life to Christian authority. It can protect free exercise without establishing religious hierarchy. It can allow faith to speak without allowing faith to rule by force.

The question is not whether Christianity belongs in American history or public debate. It plainly does. The question is whether Christian identity should determine who belongs in America now. A pluralistic democracy must answer no. No faith, however influential, should become the measure of citizenship. No religious tradition should become the owner of public life. And no nation that claims liberty of conscience should confuse Christian dominance with religious freedom.
