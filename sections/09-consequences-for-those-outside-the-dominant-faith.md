# Consequences for Those Outside the Dominant Faith

The consequences of religious political control are felt most sharply by those who stand outside the dominant faith, outside the dominant interpretation of that faith, or outside the moral categories that dominant faith politics recognizes as legitimate. Non-Christians, atheists, agnostics, women, LGBTQIA+ people, religious minorities, students, patients, migrants, and dissenting Christians may all find themselves governed by doctrines they do not hold. The harm is not only legal. It is emotional, civic, bodily, and existential. When one religious worldview becomes public authority, people outside that worldview do not experience it as inspiration. They experience it as a reduction in the size of their own freedom.

This matters because the United States is religiously diverse, even though Christianity remains culturally powerful. Pew Research Center’s 2023–24 Religious Landscape Study found that 62 percent of U.S. adults identify as Christian, while about 29 percent are religiously unaffiliated, including atheists, agnostics, and people who describe their religion as “nothing in particular” [@pew_religious_identity_2025]. That means Christian language may still function as a political default, but millions of people do not live inside that faith. They are not edge cases. They are neighbors, coworkers, students, patients, voters, parents, teachers, and citizens. A democracy cannot treat them as footnotes in someone else’s religious story.

The first consequence is unequal citizenship. When Christianity becomes the assumed moral identity of the nation, people outside Christianity may be treated as less fully rooted, less trustworthy, or less authentically American. They may be legally equal on paper while culturally framed as guests in a house someone else owns. That kind of exclusion does not always announce itself with open hostility. Sometimes it appears as a prayer at a public meeting, a Bible verse in a classroom, a campaign speech about “real Americans,” or a policy defended as “traditional values” without admitting whose tradition is being privileged. The message is quiet but clear: this public world was built with someone else in mind.

Religious minorities feel this pressure acutely. Jews, Muslims, Hindus, Buddhists, Sikhs, Indigenous spiritual communities, and others may all support religious freedom, but Christian dominance often asks them to accept a public culture where Christianity is treated as the natural center. Their holidays are optional. Their practices are accommodations. Their beliefs are diversity. Christianity is tradition. That asymmetry matters. It teaches people that some faiths belong by default while others belong by permission. Nothing says “equal liberty” like having to explain that your religion is not a scheduling inconvenience.

The religiously unaffiliated face a related problem. Atheists, agnostics, and people with no religious affiliation are often treated as if they lack moral seriousness because they do not ground ethics in God. This is one of the most persistent myths in religious politics: that morality requires religious belief, and that public life becomes dangerous when secular people gain influence. But a pluralistic democracy cannot measure citizenship by theology. A person does not need to believe in God to know the difference between cruelty and care, honesty and deception, harm and help. People can be good, generous, patient, ethical, helpful, and accommodating without needing religion as their moral supervisor. A person does not need to believe in God to care about justice, dignity, compassion, or the common good. Moral seriousness is not issued at baptism.

Women and people who can become pregnant experience religious political control most directly through laws governing reproduction. When one religious interpretation of fetal life, sexuality, and gender becomes law, the state gains power over pregnancy, miscarriage, abortion, medical emergencies, and family planning. After *Dobbs*, abortion access became radically uneven across the country; Guttmacher reports that 41 states have abortion bans in effect with limited exceptions, including 13 states with total abortion bans and 28 with gestational-duration bans [@guttmacher_abortion_bans_2026]. For people living under those laws, theological disagreement is not theoretical. It can determine whether they receive medical care, whether they must remain pregnant, whether they travel across state lines, and whether their own moral judgment matters less than a legislature’s religiously shaped assumptions.

The bodily consequence is profound. A person may not share the religious belief that life begins at conception. A doctor may understand pregnancy through medical risk rather than theology. A family may face a wanted pregnancy that becomes dangerous or nonviable. But when religious doctrine shapes law, the state can force everyone into one moral framework. The result is not simply “a policy disagreement.” It is compulsory theology experienced through the body. The law does not merely express values. It enters the clinic, the emergency room, the marriage, the bedroom, and the grief of people already facing some of the hardest decisions of their lives.

LGBTQIA+ people experience a similar conversion of religious morality into civic vulnerability. When conservative Christian politics frames queer and trans identities as threats to children, family, or civilization, LGBTQIA+ people become symbols in someone else’s moral drama. Their pronouns, books, healthcare, marriages, bathrooms, classrooms, sports teams, and family structures become political evidence. The Movement Advancement Project LGBTQIA+ curricular-law tracker documents state policies involving LGBTQIA+ inclusion and exclusion in school curricula, including “Don’t Say LGBTQ” laws, older curriculum-censorship laws, and parental opt-in or opt-out policies [@map_lgbtq_curricular_laws]. The issue is not merely whether one lesson appears in a classroom. It is whether LGBTQIA+ students and families are allowed to exist publicly without being treated as contamination.

This kind of policy produces emotional consequences that law alone does not capture. When a student hears that their family cannot be mentioned, that their identity is inappropriate, or that adults must protect other children from knowing people like them exist, the state teaches shame. When a trans person becomes the subject of legislative debate year after year, the state teaches precarity. When queer families are described as threats to religious liberty, the state teaches that their equality is someone else’s burden. A law can be written in neutral language and still land like a verdict on someone’s humanity. Bureaucracy has excellent handwriting, but it can still be cruel.

Conversion therapy belongs in this same pattern because it turns religious disapproval into a project of correction. Whether aimed at youth or adults, the premise is that queer or trans identity is a defect to be repaired rather than a life to be understood with dignity. The American Psychological Association describes sexual orientation and gender identity change efforts as ineffective and potentially harmful [@apa_conversion_therapy_evidence_2025]. The Movement Advancement Project tracks conversion therapy laws across the United States, including laws that restrict licensed mental-health providers from subjecting minors to conversion therapy practices [@map_conversion_therapy_laws_2026]. For minors, the danger is especially acute because family, church, school, and professional authority can combine into coercion. A young person may be told that love, housing, belonging, or salvation depends on becoming someone else. For adults, the pressure may be less legally direct but still spiritually and emotionally powerful: a person may be pushed toward “healing,” “restoration,” or “deliverance” by a community they cannot afford to lose. Trevor Project research has connected recent exposure to conversion therapy with higher reported suicidal thoughts and attempts among LGBTQ young people [@trevor_conversion_therapy_suicidality_2026]. The harm is not only that conversion efforts fail to change who people are. The harm is that they teach people to experience themselves as broken, sinful, shameful, or unacceptable until they become more comfortable for others. That is not care. It is rejection with a counseling vocabulary.

The stakes can become deadly. This should be said carefully, because suicide is never caused by one factor alone. But rejection, shame, homelessness, bullying, hostile laws, conversion efforts, and family abandonment can all become part of the pressure placed on already vulnerable young people. LGBTQIA+ youth who are rejected by their families or pushed out of their homes do not experience that rejection as an abstract theological position. They experience it as the loss of safety, shelter, love, and belonging. The Trevor Project’s 2025 national survey found that 36 percent of LGBTQ young people seriously considered attempting suicide in the past year, and 10 percent attempted suicide [@trevor_lgbtq_youth_mental_health_2025]. Its research on homelessness and housing instability found that LGBTQ youth who had experienced housing instability reported attempting suicide at 3.5 times the rate of LGBTQ youth who had not [@trevor_lgbtq_youth_homelessness_2022]. When parents throw out their children in the name of faith, they may call it conviction, discipline, or obedience. But a Christianity that abandons a child at the moment that child most needs love has not defended the Bible. It has betrayed the very mercy, welcome, and care for the vulnerable that the Bible repeatedly demands [@bible_matthew_25_35_40].

Pew Research Center’s 2025 survey of LGBTQ adults underscores that these are not abstract categories but lived experiences, documenting how LGBTQ Americans describe their lives, identities, challenges, and hopes in the current United States [@pew_lgbtq_experiences_2025]. The policy debate often talks about LGBTQIA+ people as issues. But people do not live as issues. They live as children trying to survive school, adults trying to keep jobs, parents trying to protect families, patients trying to receive care, and citizens trying to move through public life without becoming a campaign prop. Nothing says “family values” like turning someone else’s family into a warning label.

Students are especially vulnerable because public schools carry state authority. When religiously framed politics shapes curriculum, school libraries, history standards, sex education, or classroom discussion, students are not merely receiving information. They are being told which lives count as normal, which histories matter, and which questions are safe to ask. Religious domination in schools can harm religious minorities by making them feel like outsiders, LGBTQIA+ students by making them invisible or suspect, and secular students by implying that morality belongs to religion alone. A public school should not require a child to translate the state’s values through someone else’s theology before they can feel like they belong.

Children absorb these hierarchies quickly. A child does not need a constitutional theory to understand when their family is treated as strange, when their holiday is ignored, when their identity is whispered around, or when their religion is presented as something other than the national default. They learn belonging through repetition: whose prayers are normal, whose stories are central, whose parents are respected, whose questions are welcomed, and whose existence requires explanation. When religious dominance enters public institutions, it does not only regulate adults. It teaches children where they stand.

Patients and families also bear consequences when religious authority shapes medical access. This includes abortion and reproductive care, but it extends beyond them. Religious doctrine can influence debates over contraception, fertility treatment, end-of-life care, gender-affirming care, stem-cell research, and hospital policy. The danger is not that religious institutions have ethical commitments. They do. The danger is when those commitments govern patients who do not share them, especially when religiously affiliated healthcare systems serve the general public. A patient in crisis should not have to discover at the worst moment of their life that their medical options have been narrowed by theology they never consented to.

Dissenting Christians also suffer under dominant religious politics. Progressive Christians, queer Christians, Black church traditions, immigrant churches, peace churches, mainline Protestants, Catholics with different political commitments, and others may find their faith publicly misrepresented by a louder and more politically organized version of Christianity. When one faction claims to speak for “Christian values,” it often erases the diversity of Christian witness. Christians who emphasize poverty, racial justice, peacemaking, immigrant care, environmental stewardship, or LGBTQIA+ inclusion may be treated as fake Christians, compromised Christians, or Christians who have surrendered to secular culture. The dominant faith does not only exclude outsiders. It disciplines insiders who refuse the script.

This is one reason religious domination is so corrosive: it turns pluralism inside the faith into betrayal. Christianity itself becomes smaller when one political interpretation claims ownership of the whole tradition. The Black church’s liberation witness, Catholic social teaching, Anabaptist peace traditions, mainline Protestant reform movements, queer Christian theology, and other forms of Christian ethical life cannot be flattened into one culture-war platform without violence to the tradition’s complexity. When political Christianity claims the microphone, it often calls that unity. It is not unity. It is a volume problem.

The harm also reaches migrants and racial minorities when Christian identity is fused with national belonging. Christian nationalist politics often imagines the nation through a nostalgic moral order that is not only religious, but racialized and cultural. The “real America” invoked in these movements is often implicitly white, English-speaking, heterosexual, patriarchal, and Christian. Even when the language is formally about religion, the emotional picture is larger than doctrine. It is about who looks like they belong, whose families are treated as normal, whose grief is legible, whose accent sounds threatening, and whose presence is framed as change rather than contribution.

The emotional burden of this kind of politics is difficult to overstate. People outside the dominant faith may learn to edit themselves in public. They may hide religious symbols, avoid mentioning partners, soften political speech, avoid school conflict, travel for healthcare, change names, explain holidays, or calculate whether honesty will cost them safety. That calculation is exhausting. Equal citizenship should not require constant self-protection. Yet religious domination teaches people to ask, again and again: how much of myself can I reveal before someone else’s theology becomes my problem?

This is one of the quietest consequences of religious domination: it teaches people to manage the majority’s comfort. Those outside the dominant faith learn which parts of themselves are safe to show, which questions will make the room tense, which beliefs need softening, and which truths are better saved for private spaces. They become fluent in self-editing. The atheist learns not to sound “too atheist.” The queer student learns not to sound “too visible.” The Muslim coworker learns not to sound “too demanding.” The Jewish family learns to explain why the public calendar does not fit their life. The dissenting Christian learns that disagreement will be treated as betrayal. This is not equal belonging. It is conditional belonging, and conditional belonging is just exclusion with better manners.

This is why the phrase “religious freedom” must be examined by its effects. If religious freedom means that believers may practice, worship, speak, organize, and live according to conscience, it is essential. If religious freedom means that one group may use law to restrict the bodies, speech, education, healthcare, and families of others, it has become something else. Freedom for the dominant faith can become captivity for everyone outside it. The name remains beautiful. The function changes.

A society shaped by religious domination may still call itself free because it allows dissent in theory. But freedom in theory is thin comfort when law, culture, and public institutions repeatedly tell people that their lives are morally suspect. A person may technically be free to disagree with the dominant faith while still being forced to live under its policies. That is the difference between formal liberty and lived liberty. Formal liberty says, “You may believe what you want.” Lived liberty asks, “Can you make medical decisions, raise your family, attend school, marry, speak, work, and belong without someone else’s doctrine standing over you?”

The people outside the dominant faith do not experience religious political control as a philosophical puzzle. They experience it in paperwork, waiting rooms, classrooms, courtrooms, family arguments, school board meetings, job policies, insurance coverage, and campaign ads. They experience it when their bodies become legal territory, when their families become moral threats, when their identities become curriculum disputes, when their religions become exceptions, and when their citizenship becomes conditional. The injury is not only that rights are restricted. It is that the restriction is framed as righteousness.

That is why this issue cannot be softened into a polite disagreement about values. Values are not harmless when they are backed by law. A dominant religious group may say it is defending morality, but those outside the group may experience that defense as surveillance, exclusion, and control. The moral question must therefore be asked from the position of the governed, not only from the position of the believer. A law inspired by faith cannot be judged only by the sincerity of those who support it. It must also be judged by the freedom of those who endure it.

When faith becomes law, those outside the dominant faith become the testing ground for its power. Their bodies reveal whether religious liberty is truly liberty or merely privilege. Their classrooms reveal whether public education belongs to everyone or only to the majority’s moral imagination. Their families reveal whether equality is real or conditional. Their fear reveals what the law is doing beneath its language. And their resistance reveals the truth that domination always tries to hide: no faith has the right to make unwilling citizens live as though they belong to it.
