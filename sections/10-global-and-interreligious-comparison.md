# Global and Interreligious Comparison

Although this article focuses on Christianity, the broader danger is not Christianity alone. Any religion can become coercive when it is fused with state power, nationalist identity, or political legitimacy. The pattern appears across traditions: religious identity becomes a marker of belonging, doctrine becomes a source of law, dissent becomes moral corruption, and outsiders become threats to the imagined purity of the nation.

This comparison matters because it prevents the critique from becoming too narrow. Christianity is central to the American context because of its long cultural and political influence, but the underlying problem is structural. When a religious tradition becomes entangled with state authority, it can use the state to defend its boundaries, punish dissent, and impose its vision of morality on those beyond the faith community.

The examples differ across regions and religions. Islamic law, Hindu nationalism, Buddhist nationalism, Jewish religious nationalism, and Christian nationalism all operate within distinct histories and political contexts. They should not be collapsed into one simplistic category. But they share a recurring danger: religion becomes more than a source of identity or ethics. It becomes a governing claim.

A pluralistic society should be able to recognize this pattern without treating all religions as identical or all religious people as authoritarian. The issue is not belief itself. The issue is power. Faith becomes dangerous when it stops asking how believers should live and starts determining how everyone must live. That warning applies across traditions, including Christianity.
