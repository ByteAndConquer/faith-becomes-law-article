# Global and Interreligious Comparison

The danger of religious authority becoming political power is not unique to Christianity. Christianity is the central concern of this article because of its historical and contemporary influence in the United States, but the broader pattern is global and interreligious. Across traditions and political systems, freedom shrinks when religious identity, state authority, nationalism, and moral certainty become inseparable. Sometimes religion captures the state. Sometimes the state captures religion. Sometimes a majority faith becomes the nation’s preferred identity. The direction of control may change, but the result is familiar: belief becomes boundary, dissent becomes danger, and people outside the approved order become easier to govern, punish, or erase.

This comparison matters because criticism of politicized Christianity is often misread as hostility toward Christianity itself. But the issue is not Christianity as a faith. The issue is domination as a political form. Islam, Hinduism, Buddhism, Judaism, Christianity, and other traditions have all produced communities of compassion, learning, reform, service, beauty, resistance, and moral courage. They have also, in particular historical and political settings, been used to justify hierarchy, exclusion, censorship, violence, nationalism, and control. That does not make all religions identical, nor does it erase differences in theology, history, or political context. It shows something less comforting: no religious tradition is automatically protected from political corruption, and no government becomes harmless merely because it claims sacred purpose.

Global research on religious restrictions reinforces the seriousness of this concern. Pew Research Center has tracked government restrictions on religion and social hostilities involving religion across nearly two hundred countries and territories [@pew_global_religious_restrictions_2024; @pew_religious_restrictions_feature_2026]. The importance of this research is not merely statistical. It demonstrates that religious restriction is not an American culture-war abstraction. Around the world, governments regulate worship, privilege official traditions, punish dissent, suppress minority communities, police religious identity, and decide which forms of faith may appear in public. The vocabulary varies. The machinery does not.

Iran offers one of the clearest examples of religious authority controlling the state. The government’s official interpretation of Shi’a Islam shapes law, political legitimacy, gender regulation, public morality, and the treatment of religious minorities and dissenters. The United States Commission on International Religious Freedom (USCIRF) has documented systematic repression affecting Baha’is, Christians, Jews, Sunni Muslims, Sufis, converts, dissidents, and others who fall outside the state’s approved religious order [@uscirf_iran_2026]. In such a system, political disagreement can become religious disobedience, and religious dissent can become a threat to the government itself.

The point is not that Islam inherently produces theocracy. Muslims live within an enormous range of political, cultural, and theological traditions. The danger arises when a government claims divine legitimacy for one interpretation and equips that interpretation with police, courts, prisons, and censorship. Once that happens, the state does not merely protect religion. It determines which religion is legitimate. Believers remain free only to the extent that their belief agrees with the government’s theology. The state becomes both ruler and unofficial editor of God.

Afghanistan under the Taliban reveals the same danger in an even more intimate form. Taliban authorities enforce a severe interpretation of Sunni Islam that has been used to restrict dissent, religious minorities, women, and girls [@uscirf_afghanistan_2026]. Restrictions on education, employment, movement, dress, speech, and public participation demonstrate how quickly theological authority can enter everyday life. It does not remain in courts, sermons, or official decrees. It follows people into classrooms, workplaces, streets, homes, clothing, and bodies.

These examples make the danger of theocracy visible, but the opposite arrangement can be equally coercive. China demonstrates what happens when the state does not submit to religion but instead demands that religion submit to the state. The Chinese Communist Party is officially atheist, yet it does not simply leave religion alone. Religious groups must conform to party ideology and operate through an extensive structure of state recognition, registration, surveillance, regulation, and political supervision [@uscirf_china_state_control_2026].

China officially recognizes Buddhism, Catholicism, Islam, Protestantism, and Taoism, but legal religious activity is managed through state-controlled organizations. Religious communities that refuse this control may face harassment, detention, closure, surveillance, or other punishment. Uyghur Muslims, Tibetan Buddhists, house-church Christians, underground Catholics, Falun Gong practitioners, and other communities have faced severe repression [@uscirf_china_religious_freedom_2026]. Under the policy commonly described as the “Sinicization of religion,” religious institutions and teachings are expected to align with Chinese Communist Party leadership, political ideology, and state-defined cultural identity.

China is an important comparison because it prevents the argument from becoming too narrow. The danger is not only religion taking control of government. Government can also take control of religion. A Christian theocracy may demand that law conform to Christianity. An authoritarian communist state may demand that Christianity, Islam, Buddhism, and every other faith conform to the party. One places the sacred above the state. The other places the state above the sacred. Both arrangements deny individuals and communities the freedom to interpret, question, worship, dissent, and organize outside centralized authority.

This mirror image reveals the deeper principle: concentrated power does not care which direction the leash runs. Whether religious leaders command the state or state officials command religion, conscience becomes subordinate to an institution that claims the right to define acceptable belief. Faith is no longer free to challenge political power because political power has either absorbed it or placed it under supervision. The government may allow worship, but only after approving the guest list, editing the sermon, checking the camera feed, and making sure heaven has completed the proper registration form.

Hindu nationalism offers a different but related pattern. India is constitutionally secular, internally diverse, and home to vast and varied Hindu traditions. Yet Hindu nationalist politics has increasingly framed Indian identity through majoritarian religious and cultural terms. Human-rights organizations have documented discriminatory policies, inflammatory rhetoric, unequal enforcement, and violence affecting Muslims, Christians, and other minorities under the Bharatiya Janata Party’s political dominance [@hrw_india_religious_minorities_2026; @freedomhouse_india_2026].

Again, the issue is not Hinduism as a religion. The issue is the conversion of majority religion into a theory of national ownership. When Hindu identity is treated as the natural center of Indian nationhood, citizens outside that identity may remain legally present while becoming culturally conditional. Their citizenship may be recognized in law but questioned in rhetoric, policing, employment, housing, elections, or everyday public life. The majority religion becomes heritage. Minority religion becomes suspicion.

That conditionality is one of the most consistent features of religious nationalism. A government does not need to revoke citizenship outright to weaken belonging. It can require minorities to demonstrate loyalty, tolerate surveillance, accept unequal treatment, endure public suspicion, and watch the majority’s religion become the emotional language of the state. The minority remains a citizen, but citizenship begins to feel like probation.

Myanmar presents an especially severe example of religion, ethnicity, and nationalism becoming fused into exclusion. Buddhist nationalism and anti-Muslim rhetoric helped shape a political environment in which the Rohingya, a predominantly Muslim ethnic minority, were denied citizenship, portrayed as foreign intruders, subjected to segregation, and targeted by state violence. Myanmar’s military operations against Rohingya communities included killings, sexual violence, destruction of villages, forced displacement, and other atrocities [@ohchr_myanmar_fact_finding_genocide; @us_state_rohingya_genocide_2022].

The United States formally determined in 2022 that members of Myanmar’s military committed genocide and crimes against humanity against the Rohingya [@us_state_rohingya_genocide_2022]. A United Nations fact-finding mission concluded that senior military leaders should be investigated and prosecuted for genocide, crimes against humanity, and war crimes [@ohchr_myanmar_fact_finding_genocide]. The Gambia’s case accusing Myanmar of violating the Genocide Convention remains before the International Court of Justice, which has ordered provisional measures to protect the Rohingya while the litigation proceeds [@icj_gambia_myanmar_genocide_case]. The final legal judgment on the merits remains pending, but describing the atrocities as genocide is not merely rhetorical; it reflects formal governmental determinations, international investigations, and an active case under the Genocide Convention.

Accuracy also requires acknowledging that Rohingya armed actors have committed violence. The Arakan Rohingya Salvation Army carried out coordinated attacks against security posts in 2016 and 2017, and Amnesty International documented killings and abductions attributed to ARSA fighters, including attacks against Hindu civilians [@amnesty_arsa_abuses_2018]. Those crimes should not be denied or romanticized. A minority community does not become morally infallible because it is persecuted.

But crimes committed by an armed group do not justify collective punishment of an entire ethnic and religious population. ARSA is not every Rohingya man, woman, or child. An attack on security forces cannot justify mass rape, village destruction, citizenship denial, forced displacement, or genocidal violence against civilians. A government that responds to militants by treating an entire people as the enemy has abandoned security and embraced collective guilt. That distinction matters everywhere: accountability must follow individual responsibility, not religious or ethnic identity.

The Rohingya case therefore illustrates two truths at once. Members of a persecuted population can commit serious crimes, and that population can still be subjected to genocide. Acknowledging the first does not erase the second. Moral analysis fails when it demands perfectly innocent victims before condemning mass atrocity. Human rights are not prizes awarded only to communities whose every member behaves well. If they were, no nation on Earth would qualify.

Buddhism is often presented in Western popular culture as inherently peaceful, meditative, and detached from political violence. Myanmar exposes the limits of that simplified image. Buddhist teachings may emphasize compassion and non-harm, yet Buddhist identity can still be recruited into ethnic nationalism, fear, territorial belonging, and political exclusion. No tradition is protected by its public reputation. Sacred language can speak softly while standing beside a locked gate, a military checkpoint, or a burning village.

Israel and Palestine present another complex case in which religion, ethnicity, history, territory, trauma, security, and nationalism overlap. Any serious discussion must begin by acknowledging Jewish histories of persecution, exile, antisemitism, pogroms, and genocide. Jewish vulnerability is not invented, and Jewish self-determination cannot be evaluated honestly without recognizing the historical conditions that shaped it. Antisemitism remains real and dangerous, and criticism of Israeli policy must never become a disguise for hostility toward Jewish people.

In this discussion, “forms of Jewish religious nationalism” means political projects that invoke divine entitlement, sacred territory, or Jewish religious supremacy to justify permanent territorial domination, unequal legal status, denial of equal political rights, displacement, state-backed preferential treatment, or coercive rule over people outside the dominant religious group. The phrase does not describe Judaism, Jewish identity or political participation, Zionism in all its forms, every current associated with religious Zionism, or Israeli citizens and Jewish communities as a whole.

At the same time, historical vulnerability does not make state power immune from moral scrutiny. In the West Bank, Israeli settlers hold Israeli citizenship and civil rights that are not equally available to Palestinians living under military occupation. Palestinians face restrictions involving movement, land, construction, home demolition, and access to resources, while their political rights are constrained by both the occupation and the Palestinian Authority’s prolonged democratic failures. Settlement expansion deepens unequal territorial and legal realities [@freedomhouse_west_bank_2026; @icj_israel_occupied_palestinian_territory_2024].

The article’s concern is with religious and historical claims to land when they are joined to state power in ways that privilege one population while constraining another.

The issue is not Judaism as a faith, nor is it the existence of Jewish communal or national life. The issue is whether religious-national claims can justify permanent inequality, displacement, or domination. Jewish safety and Palestinian freedom should not be treated as mutually exclusive moral goods. A political order that protects one people by indefinitely denying equal dignity and self-determination to another has not solved historical vulnerability. It has redistributed it.

This same caution should apply across every example. Criticizing religious nationalism is not the same as attacking religious people. Antisemitism, Islamophobia, anti-Hindu hatred, anti-Christian persecution, anti-Buddhist violence, and hostility toward other religious communities are all real and dangerous. A community can be persecuted in one setting and politically dominant in another. It may deserve protection from hatred while also requiring accountability when its institutions, symbols, or political movements are used to harm others. History refuses to keep anyone in one tidy moral category.

Interreligious comparison must therefore be used carefully. The point is not to rank religions by danger or construct a scoreboard of suffering. The examples are not interchangeable. Iran is not China. China is not India. India is not Myanmar. Myanmar is not Israel and Palestine. Their histories, legal systems, religious traditions, and political conflicts differ dramatically.

The point is to identify recurring political mechanisms. A religious or ideological identity becomes a claim to national ownership. National ownership becomes a claim to legal privilege. Legal privilege becomes a mechanism for controlling outsiders and dissenters. Alternatively, a government declares itself the sole guardian of public order, forces religious communities to submit to state ideology, and punishes spiritual independence as political disobedience. In one system, religion captures government. In another, government captures religion. Either way, conscience loses.

The vocabulary changes from place to place. One movement invokes Christian civilization. Another invokes Islamic virtue. Another invokes Hindu nationhood. Another invokes Buddhist protection of the homeland. Another invokes sacred land and national security. An authoritarian state may instead invoke social harmony, national unity, modernization, or ideological loyalty. These claims emerge from different histories and cannot simply be equated. But each can become dangerous when it turns communal meaning into coercive authority.

Religious and ideological domination also tend to describe themselves as defensive. They rarely announce, “We would like to oppress people now.” They say they are protecting tradition, children, security, purity, civilization, harmony, sovereignty, national identity, or divine order. Sometimes the fear underneath those claims is sincere. Communities may fear secularization, demographic change, violence, cultural erasure, humiliation, foreign influence, or loss of political control. But fear does not automatically make coercion just. A majority’s anxiety cannot become the legal ceiling on minority freedom.

This matters for the American argument because Christian nationalism often presents itself as uniquely innocent. It claims merely to restore moral order, protect religious freedom, or honor national heritage. But global comparison shows that dominant movements nearly always describe power as protection. Majority faiths call privilege tradition. Theocracies call enforcement virtue. Nationalists call exclusion security. Authoritarian states call surveillance harmony. The language may sound reassuring, but the better question is always the same: who becomes less free when this movement gains authority?

The comparison also strengthens the article’s principle by making it universal. If Christian domination is wrong only because Christianity is involved, the argument fails. The stronger claim is that no religious or ideological authority should possess unchecked power over conscience. Christian law imposed on non-Christians is wrong. Islamic theocracy imposed on dissenters and minorities is wrong. Hindu nationalism that makes Muslims and Christians conditional citizens is wrong. Buddhist nationalism that marks Muslims as alien is wrong. Forms of Jewish religious nationalism that invoke divine entitlement to justify unequal legal status, denial of equal political rights, or permanent territorial domination are wrong. An officially atheist state that imprisons, edits, surveils, or restructures religious life is also wrong.

The principle must remain consistent or it is not a principle. It is preference wearing an academic name tag.

That consistency matters because religious freedom must protect everyone or it will eventually protect no one. Christians who support government-sponsored Christianity should consider whether they would accept state-sponsored Islam. Muslims who favor Islamic legal supremacy should consider whether they would accept Hindu nationalist rule. Secular citizens who oppose theocracy should also oppose an authoritarian government deciding which churches may exist, which imams may preach, which Buddhist leaders are legitimate, and which sacred texts may be taught.

The objection to domination cannot depend on whether the dominant worldview is familiar. The universal rule must be that no one should be forced to live under a religious or ideological authority they do not share, except where democratically enacted law is justified through public reasons and constrained by equal rights. Conscience cannot belong to the majority. It cannot belong to clergy. It cannot belong to a party. It cannot belong to the state.

The global lesson is therefore broader than religion alone. Faith can enrich public life when it speaks through conscience, service, moral imagination, solidarity, truth-telling, and resistance to cruelty. Secular government can protect freedom when it remains accountable, pluralistic, and limited. But religion fused with state power can become coercive, and a state determined to dominate religion can become equally destructive. The deeper danger is concentrated power wrapped in unquestionable moral certainty.

When political authority declares itself sacred, historically destined, culturally pure, ideologically infallible, or uniquely entitled to define belonging, freedom begins to contract. Belief becomes boundary. Identity becomes hierarchy. Difference becomes disloyalty. Outsiders become problems. Resistance becomes sin, treason, extremism, impurity, or corruption.

That is why democracies must protect religious life while limiting religious authority, and protect public order without turning the state into the owner of conscience. The goal is not a society without faith. It is a society in which faith remains free because no church, mosque, temple, monastery, party, military, ethnic majority, or government ministry has the power to make everyone else kneel.

The examples in this section differ in history, theology, culture, and political structure. They should not be flattened into moral equivalents, nor should they be treated as identical in scale or severity. Their importance lies elsewhere. They demonstrate that the danger is remarkably consistent regardless of which religion—or even which ideology—holds power. Whenever a government claims authority because it speaks for God, or whenever it claims authority over God, liberty contracts. The names change. The symbols change. The scriptures change. The flags change. The mechanism does not. Every society eventually arrives at the same question: **Who decides what every other citizen must believe, and what happens when they refuse?**
